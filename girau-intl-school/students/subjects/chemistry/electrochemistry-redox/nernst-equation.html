Nernst Equation

Understanding how concentration affects electrode potential and predicting cell voltages under non-standard conditions.

Learning Objectives

  • • State the Nernst equation and identify each term
  • • Calculate electrode potential at non-standard concentrations
  • • Predict cell potential under varying conditions
  • • Understand the relationship between E, E°, and concentration

1 What is the Nernst Equation?

The Nernst equation relates the electrode potential of a half-cell or overall cell to the standard electrode potential, temperature, and activities (approximately concentrations) of the chemical species undergoing reduction and oxidation.

The Nernst Equation

E = E° - (RT/nF) ln Q

Where:

E

Cell potential (V)

E°

Standard cell potential (V)

R

Gas constant (8.314 J/mol·K)

T

Temperature (K)

n

Number of electrons transferred

F

Faraday constant (96,500 C/mol)

Q

Reaction quotient

2 Simplified Form at 25°C

At room temperature (25°C = 298 K), the Nernst equation can be simplified by substituting the constants and converting from natural log to base-10 log:

E = E° - (0.059/n) log Q

At 25°C (298 K)

Key Point

The factor 0.059 comes from: (RT/F) × ln(10) = (8.314 × 298 / 96500) × 2.303 ≈ 0.059 V

3 Calculating the Reaction Quotient (Q)

For a general reaction: aA + bB → cC + dD

Q = [C]c [D]d / [A]a [B]b

Important Rules

  • • Solids and pure liquids have activity = 1 (not included in Q)
  • • Gases use partial pressures (in atm)
  • • Aqueous ions use molar concentrations
  • • Water as a solvent has activity ≈ 1

At Equilibrium

Q = K (equilibrium constant)

E = 0 (no driving force)

Standard Conditions

All concentrations = 1 M

Q = 1, log Q = 0, so E = E°

4 Worked Examples

Calculate the potential of a Daniel cell: Zn|Zn²⁺(0.01 M)||Cu²⁺(0.1 M)|Cu

Given: E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = -0.76 V, E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) = +0.34 V

Step 1: Calculate E°cell

E°cell = E°cathode - E°anode = +0.34 - (-0.76) = +1.10 V

Step 2: Write the cell reaction and Q

Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu

Q = [Zn²⁺] / [Cu²⁺] = 0.01 / 0.1 = 0.1

Step 3: Apply Nernst equation (n = 2)

E = E° - (0.059/n) log Q

E = 1.10 - (0.059/2) log(0.1)

E = 1.10 - (0.0295) × (-1)

E = 1.10 + 0.0295 = 1.13 V

Answer: E = 1.13 V (higher than E° because [Cu²⁺] > [Zn²⁺])

Calculate the potential of a hydrogen electrode when [H⁺] = 0.01 M and H₂ is at 1 atm.

Step 1: Write the half-reaction

2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂     E° = 0.00 V

Step 2: Calculate Q

Q = PH₂ / [H⁺]² = 1 / (0.01)² = 1 / 0.0001 = 10000

Step 3: Apply Nernst equation (n = 2)

E = E° - (0.059/n) log Q

E = 0.00 - (0.059/2) log(10000)

E = 0.00 - 0.0295 × 4

E = -0.118 V

Answer: E = -0.118 V (lower pH means lower potential)

5 Effect of Concentration on Cell Potential

Increasing Products

Q increases → log Q is positive → E < E°

Cell potential decreases

Increasing Reactants

Q decreases → log Q is negative → E > E°

Cell potential increases

Le Chatelier's Principle

The Nernst equation shows that cells "prefer" to run when reactants are concentrated and products are dilute. This aligns with Le Chatelier's principle: high reactant concentration drives the reaction forward, giving a higher cell potential.

Practice Problems

1. Calculate E for the cell: Zn|Zn²⁺(0.1 M)||Cu²⁺(0.001 M)|Cu

E°cell = 1.10 V

Show Solution

Q = [Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺] = 0.1/0.001 = 100

E = 1.10 - (0.059/2) log(100)

E = 1.10 - 0.0295 × 2

Answer: E = 1.04 V

2. What is the potential of a Cu²⁺/Cu electrode when [Cu²⁺] = 0.001 M? (E° = +0.34 V)

Show Solution

Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu

Q = 1/[Cu²⁺] = 1/0.001 = 1000

E = 0.34 - (0.059/2) log(1000)

E = 0.34 - 0.0295 × 3

Answer: E = 0.25 V

3. At what concentration of Cu²⁺ will the Cu²⁺/Cu electrode have a potential of 0.28 V? (E° = +0.34 V)

Show Solution

E = E° - (0.059/2) log(1/[Cu²⁺])

0.28 = 0.34 - 0.0295 log(1/[Cu²⁺])

0.0295 log(1/[Cu²⁺]) = 0.06

log(1/[Cu²⁺]) = 2.03

1/[Cu²⁺] = 10²·⁰³ ≈ 107

Answer: [Cu²⁺] ≈ 0.0093 M (or about 0.01 M)

Key Takeaways

  • • Nernst Equation: E = E° - (RT/nF) ln Q or E = E° - (0.059/n) log Q (at 25°C)
  • • Q uses product concentrations in the numerator, reactants in the denominator
  • • Solids and pure liquids have activity = 1
  • • Higher reactant concentration → lower Q → higher E (more driving force)
  • • At equilibrium: E = 0 and Q = K
