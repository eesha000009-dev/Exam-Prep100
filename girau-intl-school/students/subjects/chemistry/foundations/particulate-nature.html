Particulate Nature of Matter

Understanding that matter consists of tiny particles in constant motion - the foundation of chemistry.

Learning Objectives

  • • State the kinetic theory of matter
  • • Describe the arrangement and motion of particles in solids, liquids, and gases
  • • Explain changes of state using particle theory
  • • Describe evidence for the particulate nature of matter
  • • Define diffusion and explain it using kinetic theory

1 What is Matter Made Of?

The Kinetic Theory of Matter states that all matter is made up of tiny particles (atoms, molecules, or ions) that are in constant, random motion.

Key Principle:

All matter consists of particles in constant motion

Important Note

Particles are too small to be seen directly. We know they exist from indirect evidence like diffusion, Brownian motion, and changes of state.

2 States of Matter

Matter exists in three main states: solid, liquid, and gas. The state depends on how the particles are arranged and how they move.

SOLID

  • • Closely packed
  • • Regular pattern
  • • Vibrate in place
  • • Fixed shape & volume

LIQUID

  • • Close together
  • • Random arrangement
  • • Move around each other
  • • Fixed volume, takes shape of container

GAS

  • • Far apart
  • • Random arrangement
  • • Move fast in all directions
  • • No fixed shape or volume
Property Solid Liquid Gas
Particle arrangement Close, regular Close, random Far apart, random
Particle motion Vibrate only Slide past each other Move rapidly in all directions
Shape Fixed Takes container shape Fills container
Volume Fixed Fixed Variable
Compressibility Incompressible Slightly compressible Easily compressible
Energy of particles Low Medium High

3 Changes of State

When matter is heated or cooled, particles gain or lose energy. This causes changes of state.

SOLID

melting freezing

LIQUID

evaporating condensing

GAS

Melting & Evaporation

  • • Particles gain energy
  • • Move faster and further apart
  • • Overcome forces of attraction
  • • Temperature stays constant during change

Freezing & Condensation

  • • Particles lose energy
  • • Move slower and closer together
  • • Forces of attraction increase
  • • Temperature stays constant during change

Sublimation

Some substances change directly from solid to gas without becoming liquid. This is called sublimation.

Examples: Dry ice (solid CO₂), iodine, naphthalene (mothballs)

4 Evidence for Particles

Diffusion

Diffusion is the movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration.

Examples of diffusion:

  • • A drop of ink spreading in water
  • • The smell of perfume spreading through a room
  • • Tea bag color spreading in hot water

Key point: Diffusion is faster in gases than liquids because gas particles have more energy and move faster. It's slowest in solids.

Brownian Motion

Brownian motion is the random, zigzag movement of tiny particles suspended in a fluid. It was first observed by Robert Brown in 1827.

Explanation: The visible particles (like pollen) are being hit by invisible, fast-moving water molecules. The uneven bombardment causes the random motion.

Practice Problems

1. Describe the arrangement and motion of particles in a solid.

Show Answer

Arrangement: Particles are closely packed in a regular, fixed pattern.

Motion: Particles can only vibrate about their fixed positions. They cannot move from place to place.

2. Name the process: gas → liquid

Show Answer

Condensation - particles lose energy, move slower, and come closer together.

3. Why does a gas fill any container it is placed in?

Show Answer

Gas particles have high kinetic energy and move rapidly in all directions. There are weak forces between particles, so they spread out to fill all the available space.

4. What is Brownian motion and what does it prove?

Show Answer

Brownian motion is the random, zigzag movement of particles suspended in a fluid. It proves that matter consists of tiny particles in constant motion, because the visible particles are being bombarded by invisible molecules.

Key Takeaways for WAEC/JAMB/NECO

  • • All matter consists of tiny particles in constant motion
  • • Solid: close packed, vibrate only, fixed shape & volume
  • • Liquid: close but random, slide past each other, fixed volume
  • • Gas: far apart, move rapidly, no fixed shape or volume
  • • Diffusion proves particles exist and move
  • • Temperature stays constant during state changes
  • • Gases diffuse fastest; solids slowest
