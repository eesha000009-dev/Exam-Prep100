Physical & Chemical Changes

Understanding the differences between changes that affect physical form versus chemical identity.

Learning Objectives

  • • Distinguish between physical and chemical changes
  • • Identify evidence of chemical changes
  • • Give examples of each type of change
  • • Understand reversible and irreversible changes
  • • Apply knowledge to classify everyday changes

1 Physical Changes

Definition

A physical change is a change in which no new substance is formed. The substance only changes its physical state, shape, or size.

Characteristics

  • • No new substance formed
  • • Easily reversible
  • • No change in chemical composition
  • • Usually no heat/light produced

Examples

  • • Melting ice → water
  • • Boiling water → steam
  • • Dissolving salt in water
  • • Crushing a can
  • • Tearing paper

Key Point

In physical changes, the same substance is present before and after the change. Water is still H₂O whether it's ice, liquid, or steam!

2 Chemical Changes

Definition

A chemical change (chemical reaction) is a change in which one or more new substances are formed with different properties from the original substances.

Characteristics

  • • New substance(s) formed
  • • Usually difficult to reverse
  • • Change in chemical composition
  • • Often involves heat, light, or gas

Examples

  • • Burning wood → ash + gases
  • • Rusting iron → iron oxide
  • • Cooking an egg
  • • Baking a cake
  • • Souring milk

Evidence of Chemical Change:

produced

Gas

bubbles formed

Color Change

unexpected

Precipitate

solid forms

3 Physical vs Chemical Changes

Aspect Physical Change Chemical Change
New substance No Yes
Reversibility Usually easy Usually difficult
Chemical composition Unchanged Changed
Energy changes Minimal Often significant
Examples Melting, dissolving Burning, rusting

4 Classifying Common Changes

Physical Changes

  • • Melting candle wax - wax changes from solid to liquid
  • • Dissolving sugar in tea - sugar disperses but remains sugar
  • • Breaking glass - smaller pieces, still glass
  • • Freezing water - liquid becomes solid ice
  • • Magnetizing iron - magnetic property added

Chemical Changes

  • • Burning candle wax - wax reacts with oxygen → CO₂ + H₂O
  • • Baking bread - yeast fermentation produces gas
  • • Rusting iron - Fe + O₂ + H₂O → Fe₂O₃ (rust)
  • • Cooking egg - proteins denature, texture changes
  • • Burning paper - paper → ash + gases

5 Tricky Cases Explained

Dissolving is generally a physical change. The solute (like salt) spreads throughout the solvent (water) but no new substance forms. The salt can be recovered by evaporation.

However, dissolving some substances like sodium metal in water IS chemical because hydrogen gas and sodium hydroxide are produced!

No, it's physical! Boiling water turns liquid H₂O into gaseous H₂O (steam). No new substance forms - it's still water, just in gas form. The steam can condense back to liquid water.

  • Physical part: The solid wax melts (state change)
  • Chemical part: The wax vapor burns with oxygen to form CO₂ and H₂O (new substances)

The melting is reversible, but the burning is not.

Practice Problems

1. Classify as physical or chemical: (a) Melting ice, (b) Burning wood, (c) Dissolving salt in water, (d) Rusting nail

Show Solution

(a) Melting ice - Physical (state change)

(b) Burning wood - Chemical (new substances: ash, CO₂)

(c) Dissolving salt - Physical (no new substance)

(d) Rusting nail - Chemical (iron oxide forms)

2. Give three pieces of evidence that a chemical change has occurred.

Show Solution

Any three of:

  • Color change (unexpected)
  • Gas produced (bubbles)
  • Heat or light produced
  • Precipitate formed
  • Difficult to reverse

3. Explain why cooking an egg is a chemical change.

Show Solution

Cooking an egg is chemical because:

  • The proteins in the egg denature and re-form new structures
  • The texture, color, and taste change permanently
  • It cannot be reversed - you can't un-cook an egg!
  • New substances with different properties are formed

4. Is boiling an egg physical or chemical? Explain.

Show Solution

Boiling an egg is a chemical change. While the boiling water is physical, the egg itself undergoes chemical changes - the proteins denature and the egg becomes solid. This is irreversible.

Key Takeaways for WAEC/JAMB/NECO

  • • Physical: No new substance, reversible, state/shape changes only
  • • Chemical: New substance(s) formed, usually irreversible
  • • Evidence of chemical change: color, gas, heat/light, precipitate
  • • Dissolving is usually physical (but check for reactions)
  • • State changes (melting, boiling) are always physical
  • • Burning is always chemical (combustion)
