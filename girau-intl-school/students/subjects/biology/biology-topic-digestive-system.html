1 What is Digestion?

Digestion is the process by which large, insoluble food molecules are broken down into smaller, soluble molecules that can be absorbed into the bloodstream and used by the body.

Mechanical Digestion

Physical breakdown of food into smaller pieces without chemical change.

  • • Chewing (mastication) in the mouth
  • • Churning in the stomach
  • • Increases surface area for enzymes

Chemical Digestion

Enzyme-catalyzed breakdown of large molecules into smaller ones.

  • • Carbohydrates → Simple sugars
  • • Proteins → Amino acids
  • • Fats → Fatty acids & glycerol

2 Organs of the Digestive System

1. Mouth (Buccal Cavity)

  • • Teeth: Cut, tear, and grind food (mechanical digestion)
  • • Tongue: Mixes food with saliva, helps in swallowing
  • • Salivary glands: Produce saliva containing amylase (breaks down starch)
  • • Food forms a soft ball called bolus

2. Oesophagus (Gullet)

  • • Muscular tube connecting mouth to stomach
  • • Uses peristalsis (wave-like muscle contractions) to push food down
  • • No digestion occurs here - just transport

3. Stomach

  • • Muscular sac with folds called rugae
  • • Produces gastric juice containing:
    • - Pepsin: Enzyme that digests proteins
    • - Hydrochloric acid (HCl): Kills bacteria, activates pepsin
    • - Mucus: Protects stomach lining
  • • Food is churned into a semi-liquid called chyme

4. Small Intestine

  • • About 6 meters long, divided into duodenum, jejunum, ileum
  • • Duodenum: Receives bile (from liver) and pancreatic juice (from pancreas)
  • • Ileum: Main site of absorption - has villi to increase surface area
  • • Most chemical digestion and nutrient absorption happens here

5. Liver & Gall Bladder

  • • Liver: Produces bile (emulsifies fats into smaller droplets)
  • • Gall bladder: Stores bile until needed
  • • Bile is alkaline and helps neutralize stomach acid

6. Pancreas

  • • Produces pancreatic juice containing enzymes:
  • • Pancreatic amylase: Digests starch to maltose
  • • Trypsin: Digests proteins to peptides
  • • Lipase: Digests fats to fatty acids and glycerol

7. Large Intestine (Colon)

  • • Absorbs water from undigested food
  • • Contains helpful bacteria that make vitamins
  • • Forms and stores faeces before elimination
  • • Ends at the rectum and anus

3 Digestive Enzymes Summary

Enzyme Produced In Substrate Product
Salivary Amylase Salivary glands Starch Maltose
Pepsin Stomach Proteins Peptides
Pancreatic Amylase Pancreas Starch Maltose
Trypsin Pancreas Proteins/Peptides Amino acids
Lipase Pancreas Fats Fatty acids + Glycerol
Maltase Small intestine Maltose Glucose

Practice Questions

1. What is the function of bile in digestion?

Show Answer
  • Emulsifies fats - breaks large fat droplets into smaller ones
  • Increases surface area for lipase to work
  • Neutralizes acidic chyme from stomach (bile is alkaline)

2. Explain why the stomach produces mucus.

Show Answer
The stomach produces mucus to protect the stomach lining from being digested by its own hydrochloric acid and pepsin. Without mucus, the stomach would digest itself!

3. What are villi and why are they important?

Show Answer
Villi are tiny finger-like projections lining the small intestine. They:
  • Greatly increase surface area for absorption
  • Have thin walls for quick diffusion of nutrients
  • Contain blood capillaries to absorb sugars and amino acids
  • Contain lacteals to absorb fatty acids and glycerol

4. Trace the path of food through the digestive system.

Show Answer
Mouth → Oesophagus → Stomach → Small Intestine (Duodenum → Jejunum → Ileum) → Large Intestine → Rectum → Anus

Key Takeaways

  • • Digestion breaks down large molecules into small, absorbable ones
  • • Mechanical digestion = physical breakdown; Chemical digestion = enzyme action
  • • Main organs: Mouth → Oesophagus → Stomach → Small Intestine → Large Intestine
  • • Liver produces bile; Pancreas produces digestive enzymes
  • • Absorption mainly occurs in the small intestine (villi increase surface area)
