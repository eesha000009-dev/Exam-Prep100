1 The Nervous System

The nervous system is a complex network of cells that coordinates the body's actions and responses to stimuli. It works alongside the endocrine system to maintain homeostasis.

Central Nervous System (CNS)

  • • Brain - control center
  • • Spinal cord - information highway
  • • Processes information and makes decisions

Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

  • • All nerves outside the CNS
  • • Sensory neurons - carry signals TO CNS
  • • Motor neurons - carry signals FROM CNS

2 Structure of a Neuron (Nerve Cell)

A neuron is the basic unit of the nervous system. It is specialized for transmitting electrical signals called nerve impulses.

Dendrites

Short, branched extensions that receive signals from other neurons and carry them toward the cell body.

Cell Body (Soma)

Contains the nucleus and organelles. Integrates incoming signals and generates nerve impulses.

Axon

Long extension that carries nerve impulses away from the cell body. Can be up to 1 meter long!

Myelin Sheath

Fatty insulating layer around some axons. Produced by Schwann cells. Speeds up impulse transmission.

Axon Terminals

Branched endings that release neurotransmitters to pass signals to the next neuron or effector.

3 Types of Neurons

Sensory Neuron

Detects stimuli and sends signals TO the CNS

  • • Long dendrite, short axon
  • • Found in sense organs
  • • Carries info from receptors

Relay Neuron

Connects sensory and motor neurons in CNS

  • • Short dendrites and axon
  • • Found in brain and spinal cord
  • • Processes information

Motor Neuron

Carries signals FROM CNS to effectors

  • • Short dendrites, long axon
  • • Connects to muscles/glands
  • • Causes action/response

4 Synaptic Transmission

A synapse is the junction between two neurons or between a neuron and an effector. Signals are transmitted across synapses using chemicals called neurotransmitters.

  1. Impulse arrives at the axon terminal of the presynaptic neuron
  2. Calcium channels open, allowing Ca²⁺ ions to enter
  3. Synaptic vesicles fuse with the membrane and release neurotransmitters
  4. Neurotransmitters diffuse across the synaptic cleft
  5. Neurotransmitters bind to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane
  6. New impulse generated in the postsynaptic neuron
  7. Neurotransmitters broken down by enzymes or reabsorbed

Common Neurotransmitters

  • • Acetylcholine - at neuromuscular junctions, causes muscle contraction
  • • Dopamine - involved in pleasure, reward, and movement
  • • Serotonin - mood regulation, sleep, appetite
  • • Adrenaline - "fight or flight" response

5 The Reflex Arc

A reflex arc is the neural pathway that controls a reflex action - a rapid, automatic response that doesn't require conscious thought.

1. Stimulus

e.g., hot object

2. Receptor

detects stimulus

3. Sensory Neuron

to spinal cord

4. Relay Neuron

in CNS

5. Motor Neuron

to effector

6. Effector

muscle/gland

7. Response

e.g., pull away

Why Are Reflexes Important?

  • • Speed: Bypasses the brain for faster response
  • • Protection: Helps avoid injury (e.g., pulling hand from hot object)
  • • Automatic: No conscious thought needed

Practice Questions

1. What is the function of the myelin sheath?

Show Answer
The myelin sheath insulates the axon and speeds up nerve impulse transmission. It allows impulses to "jump" between gaps (Nodes of Ranvier) in a process called saltatory conduction.

3. Explain why reflex actions are faster than voluntary actions.

Show Answer
Reflex actions are faster because:
  • The impulse doesn't travel to the brain for processing
  • Only the spinal cord is involved (shorter pathway)
  • No conscious decision-making is required
  • Fewer synapses to cross

Key Takeaways

  • • The nervous system controls rapid responses through electrical impulses
  • • Neurons have dendrites (receive), cell body (process), and axon (transmit)
  • • Three types: sensory (to CNS), relay (in CNS), motor (from CNS)
  • • Synapses use neurotransmitters to transmit signals between neurons
  • • Reflex arcs provide rapid, automatic protective responses
