1 Introduction to Sensory Systems

Sensory systems allow organisms to detect changes in their environment (stimuli) and respond appropriately. They are essential for survival.

Types of Sensory Receptors

  • Photoreceptors: Detect light (eyes)
  • Mechanoreceptors: Detect pressure, touch, sound (ears, skin)
  • Chemoreceptors: Detect chemicals (nose, tongue)
  • Thermoreceptors: Detect temperature changes (skin)
  • Proprioceptors: Detect body position (muscles, joints)
  • Nociceptors: Detect pain (throughout body)

2 The Human Eye

The eye is the organ of sight. It detects light and converts it into electrical impulses that are sent to the brain.

Structure of the Eye

Outer Layer (Protective)
  • • Sclera: White outer coat, protects eye
  • • Cornea: Transparent front, refracts light
  • • Conjunctiva: Thin membrane covering sclera
Middle Layer (Vascular)
  • • Choroid: Contains blood vessels, dark pigment
  • • Ciliary body: Controls lens shape
  • • Iris: Colored part, controls pupil size

Lens

  • • Transparent, biconvex structure
  • • Focuses light on retina
  • • Changes shape for accommodation
  • • Held by suspensory ligaments

Retina

  • • Inner light-sensitive layer
  • • Contains photoreceptors
  • • Rods: dim light, black/white
  • • Cones: bright light, color vision

Other Parts

  • • Pupil: Hole in iris, lets in light
  • • Aqueous humor: Fluid in front
  • • Vitreous humor: Jelly behind lens
  • • Optic nerve: Sends signals to brain

Pathway of Light Through Eye:

Light → Cornea → Aqueous humor → Pupil → Lens → Vitreous humor → Retina → Optic nerve → Brain

3 How We See

Pupil Reflex

The iris controls the size of the pupil to regulate light entry:

  • • Bright light: Circular muscles contract → Pupil constricts → Less light enters
  • • Dim light: Radial muscles contract → Pupil dilates → More light enters

Accommodation

The lens changes shape to focus on objects at different distances:

  • • Distant objects: Ciliary muscles relax → Lens becomes thinner → Less refraction
  • • Near objects: Ciliary muscles contract → Lens becomes thicker → More refraction

Formation of Image

  • • Light rays are refracted by cornea and lens
  • • Image formed on retina is real, inverted, and smaller than the object
  • • Photoreceptors convert light to electrical impulses
  • • Optic nerve transmits impulses to visual cortex in brain
  • • Brain interprets the image correctly (right side up)

4 Eye Defects and Correction

Short-sightedness (Myopia)

Cause: Eyeball too long or lens too curved

Problem: Light focuses in front of retina

Can see: Near objects clearly

Cannot see: Distant objects clearly

Correction: Concave lens (diverging)

Long-sightedness (Hyperopia)

Cause: Eyeball too short or lens too flat

Problem: Light focuses behind retina

Can see: Distant objects clearly

Cannot see: Near objects clearly

Correction: Convex lens (converging)

Other Defects

  • Astigmatism: Uneven cornea → blurred vision at all distances
  • Presbyopia: Loss of lens elasticity with age → difficulty reading
  • Cataract: Cloudy lens → leads to blindness
  • Glaucoma: Increased pressure in eye → damages optic nerve

5 The Human Ear

The ear has two main functions: hearing and balance. It detects sound waves and head position.

Outer Ear

  • Pinna: Visible part, collects sound waves
  • Ear canal: Directs sound to eardrum
  • Earwax: Traps dust, protects ear
  • Eardrum (tympanum): Vibrates with sound

Middle Ear

  • Ossicles: Three tiny bones
  • - Malleus (hammer)
  • - Incus (anvil)
  • - Stapes (stirrup)
  • Eustachian tube: Equalizes pressure

Inner Ear

  • Cochlea: Spiral organ for hearing
  • - Contains hair cells
  • - Converts vibrations to impulses
  • Semicircular canals: Balance
  • Vestibule: Static balance

Pathway of Sound Through Ear:

Sound waves → Pinna → Ear canal → Eardrum → Ossicles → Oval window → Cochlea (fluid) → Hair cells → Auditory nerve → Brain

6 Hearing and Balance Mechanisms

Hearing Mechanism

  1. 1Sound waves are collected by pinna
  2. 2Waves travel through ear canal to eardrum
  3. 3Eardrum vibrates at same frequency as sound
  4. 4Vibrations amplified by ossicles (22×)
  5. 5Oval window transmits to cochlear fluid
  6. 6Hair cells in cochlea detect vibrations
  7. 7Impulses sent via auditory nerve to brain

Balance Mechanism

Semicircular Canals (Dynamic Balance)

Three canals at right angles, filled with fluid and hair cells. Detect rotational movements of head.

Vestibule (Static Balance)

Contains utricle and saccule with otoliths (calcium carbonate crystals). Detect head position relative to gravity.

How it works: When head moves, fluid moves and bends hair cells. This sends impulses to the cerebellum, which coordinates muscle responses to maintain balance.

7 Other Sensory Systems

Smell (Olfaction)

  • • Chemoreceptors in nasal cavity
  • • Detect airborne chemicals
  • • Olfactory nerve to brain
  • • Closely linked to taste
  • • Can detect ~10,000 different smells

Taste (Gustation)

  • • Taste buds on tongue papillae
  • • Chemoreceptors detect dissolved chemicals
  • • 5 basic tastes: sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami
  • • Different parts of tongue detect different tastes

Touch (Skin)

  • • Various receptors in skin
  • • Detect pressure, touch, temperature, pain
  • • Different body parts have different sensitivity
  • • Fingertips most sensitive

Practice Questions

1. Explain the role of the ciliary muscles in accommodation.

Show Answer
When viewing near objects: Ciliary muscles contract, reducing tension on suspensory ligaments, allowing the lens to become thicker (more convex) and increase refraction.

When viewing distant objects: Ciliary muscles relax, increasing tension on suspensory ligaments, pulling the lens thinner (less convex) and decreasing refraction.

2. What type of lens is used to correct short-sightedness and why?

Show Answer
A concave (diverging) lens is used to correct short-sightedness (myopia).

In myopia, light focuses in front of the retina because the eyeball is too long or the lens is too powerful. A concave lens diverges (spreads out) the light rays before they enter the eye, so they focus correctly on the retina instead of in front of it.

3. Describe the role of the ossicles in hearing.

Show Answer
The ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes) are three tiny bones in the middle ear that:
  • Amplify vibrations from the eardrum by about 22 times
  • Transmit vibrations from the eardrum to the oval window
  • Overcome the impedance mismatch between air and cochlear fluid
  • Protect the inner ear from loud sounds (acoustic reflex)

4. How do the semicircular canals detect rotational movement?

Show Answer
The three semicircular canals are positioned at right angles to each other. When the head rotates:
  1. The fluid (endolymph) inside the canals lags behind due to inertia
  2. This relative movement bends the hair cells in the ampulla
  3. Bent hair cells trigger nerve impulses
  4. Different canals detect rotation in different planes (horizontal, vertical, torsional)
  5. Information is sent to the cerebellum for balance coordination

Key Takeaways

  • • Eye: Cornea and lens refract light; retina contains rods (dim light) and cones (color)
  • • Accommodation: Lens changes shape to focus on near/distant objects
  • • Myopia (short-sighted): Concave lens correction; Hyperopia (long-sighted): Convex lens
  • • Ear: Ossicles amplify sound; Cochlea detects sound; Semicircular canals detect balance
  • • All sensory systems convert stimuli to electrical impulses sent to the brain
