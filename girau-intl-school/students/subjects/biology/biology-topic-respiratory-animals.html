Respiratory Systems in Animals

Learn about the different respiratory structures and mechanisms used by various animals for gas exchange. Essential topic for WAEC Biology covering adaptations and comparative anatomy.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Define respiration and explain its importance
  • Identify different respiratory structures in animals
  • Compare respiratory systems across organisms
  • Explain the mechanism of breathing in mammals

1 What is Respiration?

Definition

Respiration is the biochemical process by which living organisms break down glucose (or other organic molecules) to release energy for cellular activities. This energy is stored in ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate).

Respiratory System vs Respiration

  • • Respiratory system: Organs involved in gas exchange
  • • Cellular respiration: Chemical process in cells
  • • Breathing: Physical movement of air

Gas Exchange

  • • Oxygen (O₂) enters the blood
  • • Carbon dioxide (CO₂) leaves the blood
  • • Occurs across respiratory surfaces

Types of Cellular Respiration

Aerobic Respiration

Requires oxygen

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Energy (38 ATP)

  • • Complete breakdown of glucose
  • • Occurs in mitochondria
  • • High energy yield

Anaerobic Respiration

Without oxygen

C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂ + Energy (2 ATP)

(In yeast - alcohol fermentation)

  • • Incomplete breakdown
  • • Occurs in cytoplasm
  • • Low energy yield

2 Characteristics of Respiratory Surfaces

All respiratory surfaces share common features that make them efficient for gas exchange:

Large Surface Area

More space for diffusion to occur (e.g., alveoli have folded walls)

Thin Walls

One cell thick for short diffusion distance

Moist Surface

Gases dissolve in moisture before diffusing

Rich Blood Supply

Many capillaries to transport gases efficiently

3 Types of Respiratory Systems in Animals

A. Body Surface (Skin/Cutaneous Respiration)

Gas exchange occurs directly through the moist body surface.

Organisms:

  • • Amoeba - entire cell membrane
  • • Earthworm - moist skin
  • • Frog - skin + lungs
  • • Hydra - body wall

Adaptations:

  • • Skin must remain moist
  • • Mucus secretions
  • • Highly vascularized skin
  • • Thin epidermis

B. Gills

Specialized structures for extracting oxygen from water.

Structure of Fish Gills

Types of Gills:

  • • External gills: Project outside body (tadpoles, some molluscs)
  • • Internal gills: Inside body (fish)

Counter-Current Flow:

Blood flows opposite to water flow, maintaining concentration gradient for maximum oxygen uptake.

C. Tracheal System (Insects)

Network of tubes that carry air directly to tissues.

1
Spiracles

Openings on body surface

2
Tracheae

Main tubes with rings

3
Tracheoles

Fine tubes to cells

  • Advantages: No blood needed for gas transport; direct oxygen delivery to cells
  • Limitation: Only works for small animals due to diffusion limits

D. Lungs

Internal air-filled sacs for gas exchange with blood.

Found in:

  • • Mammals (paired lungs)
  • • Birds (air sacs + lungs)
  • • Reptiles
  • • Amphibians (simple sacs)
  • • Some fish (lungfish)

Key Features:

  • • Internal to prevent drying
  • • Alveoli increase surface area
  • • Ventilation moves air in/out
  • • Rich blood supply

4 The Mammalian Respiratory System

Structure of the Human Respiratory System

Diaphragm Below lungs Muscle that causes breathing movements

Alveoli - The Gas Exchange Surface

Each lung contains about 300 million alveoli, providing a total surface area of about 70m² for gas exchange.

Adaptations:

  • • Thin walls (one cell thick)
  • • Moist inner surface
  • • Surrounded by capillaries
  • • Large surface area
  • • Excellent blood supply

Gas Exchange in Alveoli:

O₂ diffusion: Alveolar air (high O₂) → Blood (low O₂)

CO₂ diffusion: Blood (high CO₂) → Alveolar air (low CO₂)

5 Mechanism of Breathing in Mammals

Inspiration (Breathing In)

  1. 1 Diaphragm contracts and flattens
  2. 2 Intercostal muscles contract, ribs move up and out
  3. 3 Thoracic cavity volume increases
  4. 4 Pressure in lungs decreases (below atmospheric)
  5. 5 Air rushes into the lungs

Expiration (Breathing Out)

  1. 1 Diaphragm relaxes and becomes dome-shaped
  2. 2 Intercostal muscles relax, ribs move down and in
  3. 3 Thoracic cavity volume decreases
  4. 4 Pressure in lungs increases (above atmospheric)
  5. 5 Air is forced out of the lungs

Comparison of Inspiration and Expiration

Feature Inspiration Expiration
Diaphragm Contracts (flattens) Relaxes (dome-shaped)
Intercostal muscles Contract Relax
Rib cage Moves up and out Moves down and in
Thoracic volume Increases Decreases
Lung pressure Decreases Increases

6 Worked Examples

WAEC 2020 Theory

Describe the adaptations of the alveoli for efficient gas exchange.

Solution:

  • 1. Thin walls: One cell thick for short diffusion distance, allowing rapid gas exchange
  • 2. Large surface area: About 300 million alveoli per lung, giving 70m² total surface area
  • 3. Moist inner surface: Gases dissolve in the moisture before diffusing across the membrane
  • 4. Rich blood supply: Dense network of capillaries around each alveolus maintains concentration gradient
  • 5. Good ventilation: Continuous air flow maintains concentration gradient for oxygen and carbon dioxide
WAEC 2018 Theory

Explain how a fish obtains oxygen from water using its gills.

Solution:

  1. 1. Water enters through the mouth and flows over the gill filaments
  2. 2. Each gill filament has many thin, blood-filled lamellae (plates)
  3. 3. Blood flows through the lamellae in the opposite direction to water flow (counter-current exchange)
  4. 4. This counter-current flow maintains a constant concentration gradient along the entire length
  5. 5. Oxygen diffuses from water (high concentration) into blood (low concentration)
  6. 6. Carbon dioxide diffuses from blood into water
  7. 7. Water exits through the gill slits/operculum
