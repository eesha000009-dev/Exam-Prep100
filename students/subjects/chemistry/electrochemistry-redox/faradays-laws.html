Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

Understanding the quantitative relationship between electricity and chemical change in electrolysis.

Learning Objectives

  • • State Faraday's First and Second Laws of Electrolysis
  • • Calculate the mass of substances deposited during electrolysis
  • • Use the Faraday constant (F = 96,500 C mol⁻¹) in calculations
  • • Solve problems involving charge, current, time, and mass

1 What are Faraday's Laws?

Michael Faraday (1791-1867) was an English scientist who discovered the fundamental laws governing electrolysis. His laws establish the quantitative relationship between the amount of electricity passed through an electrolyte and the amount of substance deposited or liberated at the electrodes.

Key Concept:

Faraday's laws allow us to predict exactly how much of a substance will be produced during electrolysis if we know the current and time, or to calculate how much current is needed to produce a specific amount of substance.

2 Faraday's First Law

First Law Statement

The mass of a substance deposited or liberated at an electrode during electrolysis is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity (charge) passed through the electrolyte.

m ∝ Q

m = Z × Q

Where: m = mass (g), Q = charge (C), Z = electrochemical equivalent

Since Q = I × t (Charge = Current × Time):

m = Z × I × t

m = mass deposited (grams)

Z = electrochemical equivalent (g C⁻¹)

I = current (amperes)

t = time (seconds)

Electrochemical Equivalent (Z)

The electrochemical equivalent is the mass of a substance deposited by 1 coulomb of electricity.

Z = M / (n × F)

Where M = molar mass, n = number of electrons transferred, F = Faraday constant (96,500 C mol⁻¹)

3 Faraday's Second Law

Second Law Statement

When the same quantity of electricity is passed through different electrolytes, the masses of substances deposited are proportional to their chemical equivalents (equivalent weights).

m₁/m₂ = E₁/E₂

Where E = equivalent weight = M/n (molar mass ÷ valence)

Combined Formula:

m = (M × I × t) / (n × F)

m = mass (g)

M = molar mass (g/mol)

I = current (A)

t = time (s)

n = electrons transferred

F = 96,500 C/mol

Ion Molar Mass (g/mol) n (electrons) Z (g/C)
Cu²⁺ 63.5 2 3.29 × 10⁻⁴
Ag⁺ 108 1 1.12 × 10⁻³
Al³⁺ 27 3 9.33 × 10⁻⁵
Zn²⁺ 65.4 2 3.39 × 10⁻⁴

4 Worked Examples

Calculate the mass of copper deposited when a current of 2 A flows through copper(II) sulfate solution for 30 minutes.

Step 1: Identify the given values

I = 2 A, t = 30 min = 30 × 60 = 1800 s
M(Cu) = 63.5 g/mol, n = 2, F = 96,500 C/mol

Step 2: Apply the formula

m = (M × I × t) / (n × F)

Step 3: Substitute and calculate

m = (63.5 × 2 × 1800) / (2 × 96,500)
m = 228,600 / 193,000
m = 1.18 g

Answer: 1.18 g of copper is deposited

How long must a current of 5 A be passed through molten silver chloride to deposit 10.8 g of silver?

Step 1: Identify values

m = 10.8 g, I = 5 A
M(Ag) = 108 g/mol, n = 1, F = 96,500 C/mol

Step 2: Rearrange formula for time

t = (m × n × F) / (M × I)

Step 3: Calculate

t = (10.8 × 1 × 96,500) / (108 × 5)
t = 1,042,200 / 540
t = 1930 seconds = 32.2 minutes

Answer: 32.2 minutes (or 1930 seconds)

The same current is passed through solutions of CuSO₄ and AgNO₃ connected in series. If 1.27 g of copper is deposited, what mass of silver is deposited?

Step 1: Apply Second Law

m(Ag)/m(Cu) = E(Ag)/E(Cu)

Step 2: Calculate equivalent weights

E(Ag) = M/n = 108/1 = 108 g/equiv
E(Cu) = M/n = 63.5/2 = 31.75 g/equiv

Step 3: Calculate mass of silver

m(Ag)/1.27 = 108/31.75
m(Ag) = 1.27 × (108/31.75)
m(Ag) = 4.32 g

Answer: 4.32 g of silver is deposited

Practice Problems

1. Calculate the mass of silver deposited when a current of 0.5 A flows through silver nitrate solution for 1 hour.

Show Solution

m = (108 × 0.5 × 3600) / (1 × 96,500)

m = 194,400 / 96,500

Answer: 2.01 g

2. What current is needed to deposit 5 g of copper from CuSO₄ solution in 2 hours?

Show Solution

I = (m × n × F) / (M × t)

I = (5 × 2 × 96,500) / (63.5 × 7200)

I = 965,000 / 457,200

Answer: 2.11 A

3. How many Faradays are required to deposit 54 g of aluminium from molten Al₂O₃?

Show Solution

Moles of Al = 54/27 = 2 mol

Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al

Each mole of Al needs 3 Faradays

Faradays = 2 × 3 = 6 F

4. When 2 A was passed through CuSO₄ solution for 45 minutes, 1.77 g of copper was deposited. Calculate the value of the Faraday constant.

Show Solution

F = (M × I × t) / (m × n)

F = (63.5 × 2 × 2700) / (1.77 × 2)

F = 342,900 / 3.54

Answer: F ≈ 96,864 C/mol (close to the accepted value of 96,500)

Key Takeaways

  • • First Law: Mass deposited ∝ Charge passed (m = Z × Q)
  • • Second Law: Same charge gives masses proportional to equivalent weights
  • • Master Formula: m = (M × I × t) / (n × F)
  • • Faraday Constant: F = 96,500 C mol⁻¹ (charge of 1 mole of electrons)
  • • 1 Faraday deposits 1 equivalent of any substance
