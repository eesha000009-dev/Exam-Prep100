Basic Principles

Understanding the fundamental concepts of electrolysis

Key Terms

Electrolyte

An ionic compound that conducts electricity when molten or in solution

Electrodes

The conductors through which electricity enters or leaves the electrolyte

Anode (+)

The positive electrode where oxidation occurs

Cathode (-)

The negative electrode where reduction occurs

Conductors: Electrons vs. Ions

How a substance conducts electricity is key for predicting electrolysis behaviour.

  • Metals (e.g., Na, Cu, Mg): conduct via mobile electrons. The metal atoms themselves do not move.
  • Electrolytes (e.g., molten PbI₂, aqueous NaOH): conduct via mobile ions (cations and anions); they must be molten or in solution.
  • Non-conductors: ionic solids (ions locked in a lattice) and molecular substances (neutral molecules) do not conduct electricity in the solid state.

Electrolytes & Electrodes

Electrolytes can be strong (fully ionise in water, good conductors) or weak (partially ionise). Electrodes are either inert (graphite, Pt) or active (e.g., copper which may dissolve).

Mnemonic: PANIC — Positive Anode, Negative Is Cathode. Anode = oxidation, Cathode = reduction.

Migration & Discharge

Cations (positive ions like Na⁺, Cu²⁺, H⁺) migrate to the cathode and are reduced (gain electrons). Anions (negative ions like Cl⁻, Br⁻, OH⁻) migrate to the anode and are oxidized (lose electrons).

At the cathode (reduction): e.g., Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu; 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂.

At the anode (oxidation): e.g., 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻; 4OH⁻ → 2H₂O + O₂ + 4e⁻.

Rules: Who Gets Discharged?

In aqueous electrolysis water supplies H⁺ and OH⁻; only one species is discharged at each electrode. Decide the product by applying these rules:

  1. Electrochemical series: at the cathode the least reactive (lowest) ion is discharged. Highly reactive metals will not be deposited from aqueous solutions (H⁺ is often discharged instead).
  2. Concentration: at the anode OH⁻ normally gives O₂, but concentrated halide ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) may be preferentially oxidized to halogen gas.
  3. Electrode nature: inert electrodes provide a surface only; active metal anodes can dissolve (e.g., copper anode → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻).

Calculations in Electrolysis

Understanding and applying Faraday's Laws in electrolysis calculations

Faraday's First Law

The mass of a substance deposited or liberated at an electrode is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity passed through the electrolyte.

Key Formula

m = (It × M)/(nF)

Worked example: Mass of lead produced

Calculate the mass of lead produced when a current of 2.0 A passes for 30 minutes during the electrolysis of PbBr2 (Pb2+ + 2e- → Pb).

  • I = 2.0 A
  • t = 30 min = 30 × 60 = 1800 s
  • M (Pb) = 207.2 g mol-1
  • n = 2
  • F = 96,500 C mol-1

Calculation:

m = (2.0 × 1800 × 207.2) / (2 × 96500) ≈ 3.87 g

So about 3.87 g of lead will be deposited.

Key Definitions

  • Electrolyte — A substance that produces ions in solution or when molten and therefore conducts electricity.
  • Electrode — A conductor (anode or cathode) through which current enters or leaves the electrolyte.
  • Anode — The positive electrode where oxidation occurs (anions give up electrons).
  • Cathode — The negative electrode where reduction occurs (cations gain electrons).
  • Primary cell — A non-rechargeable electrochemical cell. Secondary cell — A rechargeable cell.

Short Q&A (extracted from common exam prompts)

Why does pure water not conduct well?

Pure water has a very low concentration of H3O+/OH- ions (autoionisation is 10-7 mol L-1), so it is a poor conductor. Adding an electrolyte increases ion concentration and conductivity.

Why does solid NaCl not conduct?

Ions in a solid ionic lattice are fixed in place and cannot move; therefore solid NaCl does not conduct electricity. When molten or dissolved, ions are free to move and conduct.

Predicting products at electrodes (short rules)

  • Cathode (reduction): the least reactive metal ion (or H+ if metal is more reactive) is discharged.
  • Anode (oxidation): halide ions (Cl-, Br-, I-) are discharged in preference to OH- when present at sufficient concentration (concentrated halide → halogen gas). If halide is absent or dilute, OH- is oxidized to O2.

Effect of concentration (dilute vs concentrated NaCl)

Concentrated brine (high [Cl-]) → chlorine gas at anode. Dilute NaCl (low [Cl-- oxidation), hydrogen usually at cathode (from H+/water).

Worked examples (from your photos)

1) Electrolysis of molten lead(II) bromide (PbBr2)

Molten ionic compound — only ions present. At the cathode Pb2+ is reduced; at the anode Br- is oxidized.

Cathode: Pb2+ + 2e- → Pb (s)

Anode: 2Br- → Br2 (g) + 2e-

3) Half-equations and observations for common cells

Electrolysis of aqueous NaCl (dilute):

Cathode: 2H2O + 2e- → H2 + 2OH- (hydrogen produced). Anode: 4OH- → O2 + 2H2O + 4e- (oxygen produced).

Electrolysis of concentrated NaCl (brine): Anode: 2Cl- → Cl2 + 2e- (chlorine produced). Cathode: H2 as above (unless a less reactive metal ion is present).

4) Graphite electrodes — advantages & disadvantages

  • Advantages: inert (do not take part chemically in many electrolyses), inexpensive, good conductor.
  • Disadvantages: can oxidise slowly at the anode (forming CO/CO2 or causing electrode wear), may contaminate product in some cases and are brittle.
