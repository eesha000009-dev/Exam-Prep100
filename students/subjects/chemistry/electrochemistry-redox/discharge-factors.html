1 Introduction to Selective Discharge

During electrolysis, when an electrolyte contains more than one type of cation or anion, which ion gets discharged? The answer depends on several factors working together.

The Key Question

If an electrolyte contains multiple ions (e.g., CuSO₄ solution has Cu²⁺, H⁺, SO₄²⁻, OH⁻), which ions will be discharged at each electrode?

At the Cathode (Reduction)

Cations (positive ions) gain electrons

Mⁿ⁺ + ne⁻ → M

At the Anode (Oxidation)

Anions (negative ions) lose electrons

Aⁿ⁻ → A + ne⁻

2 Factor 1: Position in the Electrochemical Series

Key Rule

At the cathode: Cations below H⁺ in the series (Cu²⁺, Ag⁺, Au³⁺) are discharged instead of H⁺. If the cation is above H⁺, then H⁺ from water is discharged instead.

At the anode: Halide ions are discharged before OH⁻. If no halide is present, OH⁻ from water is discharged.

3 Factor 2: Concentration of Ions

The concentration of ions can override the electrochemical series predictions, especially for chloride ions.

Concentration Effect on Chloride

Low concentration Cl⁻: OH⁻ is discharged (oxygen produced)

High concentration Cl⁻: Cl⁻ is discharged (chlorine produced)

Dilute NaCl Solution

Cathode: H⁺ discharged → H₂ gas (H⁺ from water)

Anode: OH⁻ discharged → O₂ gas

Overall: Na⁺ and Cl⁻ remain in solution

Concentrated NaCl Solution

Cathode: H⁺ discharged → H₂ gas

Anode: Cl⁻ discharged → Cl₂ gas

Overall: NaOH remains in solution

Important Note

The concentration effect is most significant for chloride. For very dilute solutions, OH⁻ is discharged. For concentrated chloride solutions, Cl⁻ is discharged. This is the basis of the chlor-alkali industry.

4 Factor 3: Nature of Electrodes

The electrode material affects which products are formed. Inert electrodes (Pt, C/Graphite) do not participate in reactions. Reactive electrodes can be oxidized.

Inert Electrodes

  • • Platinum (Pt)
  • • Graphite (Carbon)
  • • Do not react during electrolysis
  • • Only transfer electrons
  • • Products depend on ions present

Reactive (Active) Electrodes

  • • Copper, Silver, etc.
  • • Anode can dissolve
  • • Cathode can gain metal coating
  • • Used in electroplating
  • • Anode reaction: M → Mⁿ⁺ + ne⁻

Electroplating with Copper

  • • Anode: Pure copper → dissolves (Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻)
  • • Cathode: Object to be plated → copper deposits (Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu)
  • • Electrolyte: CuSO₄ solution
  • • The concentration of Cu²⁺ in solution remains constant

5 Factor 4: Overpotential

Overpotential is the extra voltage needed beyond the theoretical value to discharge an ion at an electrode. It depends on the electrode material and the ion being discharged.

Effects of Overpotential

  • • O₂ and H₂ have high overpotential on many electrodes
  • • This can affect which ion is discharged
  • • Platinum has low overpotential for H₂
  • • Mercury has high overpotential for H₂

Practical Example

When electrolysing concentrated NaCl with a mercury cathode, Na⁺ is discharged instead of H⁺ because H₂ has very high overpotential on mercury. This produces sodium amalgam (Na/Hg) used industrially.

Practice Questions

1. Predict the products of electrolysis of dilute CuSO₄ solution using inert electrodes.

Show Answer
Cathode: Copper (Cu) - Cu²⁺ is below H⁺ in the electrochemical series
Anode: Oxygen (O₂) - SO₄²⁻ is above OH⁻ in the series

Equations:
Cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu
Anode: 4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻

2. Explain why chlorine is produced at the anode when concentrated NaCl is electrolysed, but oxygen is produced when dilute NaCl is used.

Show Answer
Concentrated NaCl: The high concentration of Cl⁻ ions makes them discharge preferentially, producing Cl₂ gas. The concentration effect overrides the fact that OH⁻ should be discharged based on the electrochemical series.

Dilute NaCl: The low concentration of Cl⁻ means OH⁻ ions (from water) are discharged instead, producing O₂ gas. Without high concentration, the electrochemical series order is followed.

3. What happens at the electrodes during copper purification by electrolysis?

Show Answer
Anode (Impure copper): Copper dissolves
Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻

Cathode (Pure copper strip): Copper deposits
Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu

Result: Impurities fall to the bottom as "anode sludge" and pure copper is deposited on the cathode.

4. Predict the products of electrolysis of molten PbBr₂ and aqueous PbBr₂.

Show Answer
Molten PbBr₂:
• Cathode: Lead (Pb) - only Pb²⁺ present
• Anode: Bromine (Br₂) - only Br⁻ present

Aqueous PbBr₂:
• Cathode: Lead (Pb) - Pb²⁺ below H⁺
• Anode: Bromine (Br₂) - Br⁻ is a halide, discharged before OH⁻

Key Takeaways

  • • Electrochemical series determines discharge order when concentration is similar
  • • At cathode: Cations below H⁺ are discharged instead of H⁺
  • • At anode: Halides are discharged before OH⁻; high [Cl⁻] overrides this
  • • Inert electrodes (Pt, C) don't react; active electrodes participate in reactions
  • • Overpotential affects discharge of gases, especially H₂ and O₂
