Electrolysis Basics

Understanding the principles of electrolysis, electrolytic cells, and industrial applications.

Learning Objectives

  • • Define electrolysis and identify its key components
  • • Distinguish between electrolytic and electrochemical cells
  • • Predict products at the anode and cathode
  • • Explain industrial applications of electrolysis

1 What is Electrolysis?

Electrolysis is the process of using electrical energy to drive a non-spontaneous chemical reaction. It involves passing an electric current through an electrolyte (molten or aqueous ionic compound) to decompose it into its elements.

Key Components:

  • Electrolyte: An ionic compound (molten or in solution) that conducts electricity
  • Anode (+): Positive electrode where oxidation occurs
  • Cathode (-): Negative electrode where reduction occurs
  • Power source: Battery or DC power supply that provides electrical energy

Schematic diagram of an electrolytic cell

2 What Happens at the Electrodes?

At the Cathode (-)

  • • Reduction occurs (gain of electrons)
  • • Cations migrate here
  • • Cations gain electrons and become neutral
  • • For molten electrolytes: Metal forms
  • • For aqueous solutions: Check electrochemical series
Mn+ + ne- → M

At the Anode (+)

  • • Oxidation occurs (loss of electrons)
  • • Anions migrate here
  • • Anions lose electrons and become neutral
  • • For molten electrolytes: Non-metal forms
  • • For aqueous: Check discharge preference
An- → A + ne-

Predict the products when molten sodium chloride is electrolyzed.

Step 1: Identify the ions present

Na+ (cations) and Cl- (anions)

Step 2: At the cathode (reduction)

Na+ + e- → Na (sodium metal)

Step 3: At the anode (oxidation)

2Cl- → Cl2 + 2e- (chlorine gas)

Products:

Cathode: Sodium metal (Na)

Anode: Chlorine gas (Cl₂)

3 Electrolysis of Aqueous Solutions

Important: In aqueous solutions, water molecules dissociate into H+ and OH- ions, which compete with the electrolyte ions for discharge.

Discharge Preferences (Selective Discharge)

At the Cathode:

The ion lower in the electrochemical series is discharged preferentially.

If metal is above Zn: H₂ is produced instead

If metal is below Zn: Metal is deposited

At the Anode:

Order of discharge preference:

1. Halides (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻)

2. OH⁻ → O₂

3. Other anions (not discharged)

Predict the products when dilute sodium chloride solution is electrolyzed.

Ions present: Na+, H+ (cations); Cl-, OH- (anions)

At Cathode: H+ is discharged (Na is more reactive than H)

2H+ + 2e- → H2 (hydrogen gas)

At Anode: OH- is discharged (Cl⁻ concentration is low)

4OH- → 2H2O + O2 + 4e-

Products:

Cathode: Hydrogen gas (H₂)

Anode: Oxygen gas (O₂)

NaOH remains in solution (alkaline)

4 Industrial Applications

Extraction of Metals

Aluminium, sodium, and magnesium are extracted by electrolysis of their molten ores.

Electroplating

Coating objects with a thin layer of metal (e.g., silver plating, gold plating) for protection or decoration.

Purification of Copper

Impure copper is made the anode; pure copper deposits at the cathode. Impurities fall as anode sludge.

Manufacturing

Production of chlorine, hydrogen, and sodium hydroxide from brine (chlor-alkali process).

Practice Problems

1. What are the products of electrolyzing molten lead(II) bromide, PbBr₂?

Show Solution

Cathode: Pb²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Pb (lead metal)

Anode: 2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻ → Br₂ (bromine gas)

2. Predict the products when concentrated NaCl(aq) is electrolyzed.

Show Solution

Cathode: 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ (hydrogen)

Anode: 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻ → Cl₂ (chlorine)

Note: At high Cl⁻ concentration, chloride is discharged at anode.

3. Why is cryolite used in the extraction of aluminium?

Show Solution

Cryolite (Na₃AlF₆) is used to:

  • Lower the melting point of Al₂O₃ (from 2050°C to about 950°C)
  • Reduce energy costs
  • Improve conductivity of the electrolyte

Key Takeaways

  • • Electrolysis uses electrical energy to decompose compounds
  • • Reduction occurs at cathode (gain electrons); Oxidation at anode (lose electrons)
  • • In aqueous solutions, H⁺ and OH⁻ compete with electrolyte ions
  • • The electrochemical series helps predict discharge products
  • • Applications include metal extraction, electroplating, and chemical manufacturing
