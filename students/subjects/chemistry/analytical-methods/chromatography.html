Chromatography

A separation technique used to separate and analyze mixtures based on their different affinities for a stationary and mobile phase.

Learning Objectives

  • • Explain the principles of chromatography
  • • Describe paper chromatography and thin-layer chromatography (TLC)
  • • Calculate Rf values and interpret chromatograms
  • • Explain applications of chromatography in analysis

1 What is Chromatography?

Chromatography is a technique used to separate the components of a mixture based on their different rates of movement through a stationary phase under the influence of a mobile phase.

Stationary Phase

The phase that doesn't move. Components can adsorb (stick) to this phase.

Examples: Paper, silica gel, alumina

Mobile Phase

The phase that moves through/over the stationary phase, carrying components.

Examples: Solvents like water, ethanol, propanone

Key Principle

Components with higher affinity for the stationary phase move slower. Components with higher solubility in the mobile phase move faster.

2 Paper Chromatography

Procedure:

  1. Draw a pencil baseline near the bottom of the chromatography paper
  2. Place a small spot of the mixture on the baseline and let it dry
  3. Suspend the paper in a beaker with solvent below the baseline
  4. Cover with a lid to keep the atmosphere saturated with solvent vapour
  5. Allow the solvent to rise by capillary action
  6. Remove the paper when the solvent front nears the top
  7. Mark the solvent front and let the paper dry
  8. If spots are colorless, use locating agents (e.g., ninhydrin for amino acids, UV light)

Important Points

  • • Use pencil for the baseline - ink would dissolve and interfere!
  • • The spot must be above the solvent level initially
  • • A concentrated spot (small) gives better separation
  • • Different solvents give different separations

3 Rf Values (Retention Factor)

The Rf value identifies components by comparing how far they travel relative to the solvent front.

Rf = distance traveled by substance / distance traveled by solvent

About Rf Values

  • • Rf values are always between 0 and 1
  • • Rf value is characteristic for a substance in a given solvent
  • • Compare Rf values with known substances to identify components
  • • Rf depends on the solvent used - always specify the solvent!

4 Types of Chromatography

Paper Chromatography

Stationary phase: Paper (cellulose)
Mobile phase: Solvent
Use: Separating dyes, amino acids, sugars

Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC)

Stationary phase: Silica gel or alumina on glass/plastic plate
Mobile phase: Solvent
Use: Faster than paper, better separation

Gas Chromatography (GC)

Stationary phase: Liquid coated on solid support in column
Mobile phase: Inert gas (He, N₂)
Use: Separating volatile compounds, forensic analysis

Practice Questions

1. A substance travels 4.2 cm while the solvent travels 6.0 cm. Calculate the Rf value.

Show Solution

Rf = distance of substance / distance of solvent

Rf = 4.2 cm / 6.0 cm = 0.70

2. Why is it important that the starting spot is above the solvent level?

Show Solution
If the spot were below the solvent level, the sample would dissolve directly into the solvent at the bottom instead of being carried up the paper by capillary action. This would ruin the separation.

3. State two applications of chromatography.

Show Solution
  • Forensic analysis - identifying inks, drugs, poisons
  • Food industry - detecting additives and contaminants
  • Medicine - analyzing blood/urine samples
  • Environmental testing - detecting pollutants

Key Takeaways

  • • Chromatography separates mixtures based on different affinities for stationary/mobile phases
  • • Rf = distance of substance ÷ distance of solvent (always between 0-1)
  • • Components with higher solubility in mobile phase move faster
  • • Used in forensics, food testing, medicine, and environmental analysis
