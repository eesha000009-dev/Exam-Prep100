Acids, Bases and Salts

Understanding the fundamental concepts of acids, bases, and the salts they produce.

Learning Objectives

  • • Define acids and bases using different theories
  • • Identify properties of acids and bases
  • • Describe the pH scale and its significance
  • • Explain neutralization reactions
  • • Classify different types of salts

1 Definitions of Acids and Bases

Arrhenius Theory (1884)

Acid

A substance that produces H+ ions (protons) when dissolved in water.

Example: HCl → H+ + Cl-

Base

A substance that produces OH- ions (hydroxide ions) when dissolved in water.

Example: NaOH → Na+ + OH-

Limitation: Only applies to aqueous solutions; cannot explain basicity of NH₃.

Brønsted-Lowry Theory (1923)

Acid

A proton donor (H+ donor)

Example: HCl + H₂O → H₃O+ + Cl-

Base

A proton acceptor (H+ acceptor)

Example: NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄+ + OH-

Advantage: Explains basicity of ammonia and applies to non-aqueous solutions.

2 Properties of Acids and Bases

Properties of Acids

  • • Sour taste (e.g., lemon juice, vinegar)
  • • Turn blue litmus paper RED
  • • React with metals to produce hydrogen gas
  • • React with carbonates to produce CO₂
  • • Conduct electricity in solution
  • • pH value less than 7
  • • React with bases to form salt and water

Properties of Bases

  • • Bitter taste (e.g., soap, baking soda)
  • • Turn red litmus paper BLUE
  • • Soapy/slippery feel
  • • React with acids to form salt and water
  • • Conduct electricity in solution
  • • pH value greater than 7
  • • Can neutralize acids

3 The pH Scale

The pH scale is a measure of the acidity or alkalinity of a solution. It ranges from 0 to 14, with 7 being neutral.

0 (Acidic) 7 (Neutral) 14 (Alkaline)

pH = -log[H+]

Lower pH = Higher [H+] = More acidic

pH Substance Nature
1Stomach acid (HCl)Strongly acidic
2-3Lemon juice, vinegarAcidic
4-5Tomatoes, coffeeWeakly acidic
7Pure water, bloodNeutral
8-9Baking soda, seawaterWeakly basic
10-11Milk of magnesiaBasic
13-14NaOH, bleachStrongly basic

4 Neutralization Reactions

Neutralization is the reaction between an acid and a base to form salt and water.

General Equation:

Acid + Base → Salt + Water

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

5 Types of Salts

Normal Salts

Formed when all H⁺ ions of an acid are replaced. No replaceable H⁺ remains.

Examples: NaCl, K₂SO₄, CaCO₃

Acid Salts

Formed when only some H⁺ ions of a polyprotic acid are replaced.

Examples: NaHSO₄, NaHCO₃, KH₂PO₄

Basic Salts

Formed when not all OH⁻ ions of a base are replaced.

Examples: Pb(OH)Cl, Bi(OH)₂NO₃

Double Salts

Contain two different cations in a definite proportion.

Examples: KAl(SO₄)₂·12H₂O (potash alum)

Practice Problems

1. Define an acid according to Brønsted-Lowry theory.

Show Answer

An acid is a proton (H⁺) donor.

2. Write the balanced equation for the reaction between HNO₃ and Ca(OH)₂.

Show Answer

2HNO₃ + Ca(OH)₂ → Ca(NO₃)₂ + 2H₂O

3. Calculate the pH of a solution with [H⁺] = 0.01 mol/dm³

Show Answer

pH = -log[H⁺] = -log(0.01) = -log(10⁻²) = 2

4. Why is NaHCO₃ called an acid salt?

Show Answer

NaHCO₃ is an acid salt because it still contains one replaceable hydrogen ion (H⁺) from the parent acid H₂CO₃. It can further react with bases to form Na₂CO₃.

Key Takeaways

  • • Acids are proton donors (Brønsted-Lowry); bases are proton acceptors
  • • pH scale ranges from 0-14; pH 7 is neutral
  • • Neutralization: Acid + Base → Salt + Water
  • • Litmus is a common indicator: acids turn blue litmus red; bases turn red litmus blue
  • • Salts are classified as normal, acid, basic, or double salts
