What is Ionic Bonding?

An ionic bond is the electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions. It is formed when one or more electrons are transferred from a metal atom to a non-metal atom.

Key Concept: Octet Rule

Atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons to achieve a full outer shell of 8 electrons (like noble gases). This stable arrangement is called an octet.

Writing Ionic Formulae

The formula of an ionic compound shows the simplest ratio of ions that gives a net charge of zero.

Rules for Writing Formulae

  1. Write the symbols of the ions (cation first)
  2. Write the charges as superscripts
  3. Swap the charges (drop the signs)
  4. Simplify if necessary
  5. Use brackets for polyatomic ions when subscript > 1

Worked Examples

Sodium chloride:

Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl

1:1 ratio, charges cancel

Magnesium oxide:

Mg²⁺ + O²⁻ → MgO

1:1 ratio, charges cancel

Sodium oxide:

Na⁺ + O²⁻ → Na₂O

Need 2 Na⁺ to balance O²⁻

Calcium chloride:

Ca²⁺ + Cl⁻ → CaCl₂

Need 2 Cl⁻ to balance Ca²⁺

Aluminium oxide:

Al³⁺ + O²⁻ → Al₂O₃

Cross-multiply: 2×3 = 6, 3×2 = 6

Calcium hydroxide:

Ca²⁺ + OH⁻ → Ca(OH)₂

Brackets needed for polyatomic ion with subscript 2

Giant Ionic Lattice Structure

Ionic compounds form a giant ionic lattice - a regular, repeating 3D arrangement of positive and negative ions held together by strong electrostatic forces.

Cation (+)
Anion (−)

Key Features of Ionic Lattices

  • Each ion is surrounded by ions of opposite charge
  • Strong electrostatic forces in all directions
  • Large amounts of energy needed to break the lattice
  • No individual molecules - just one giant structure

Properties of Ionic Compounds

High Melting & Boiling Points

Strong electrostatic forces between ions require large amounts of energy to overcome.

NaCl: m.p. 801°C

MgO: m.p. 2852°C

Soluble in Water

Water molecules can surround and separate ions, breaking down the lattice.

NaCl dissolves easily in water

AgCl is insoluble (exception)

Conduct Electricity When Molten or Dissolved

Ions are free to move and carry charge. Solid ionic compounds do NOT conduct.

Molten NaCl: ✓ conducts

NaCl solution: ✓ conducts

Solid NaCl: ✗ does not conduct

Brittle

If layers shift, like-charged ions align and repel, causing the crystal to crack.

Hit salt crystals → they shatter

Practice Problems

1. Write the formula for aluminium sulphate.

Al³⁺ and SO₄²⁻

Cross-multiply: Al₂(SO₄)₃

Formula: Al₂(SO₄)₃

Note: Brackets needed because SO₄ is a polyatomic ion with subscript 3

2. Explain why solid sodium chloride does not conduct electricity, but molten sodium chloride does.

In solid NaCl, the ions are locked in fixed positions in the lattice structure. They cannot move freely to carry charge.

When molten, the ionic lattice breaks down. The ions become free to move throughout the liquid, allowing them to carry electric charge and conduct electricity.

3. Draw a dot and cross diagram for magnesium oxide (MgO).

Mg (2,8,2) loses 2 electrons → Mg²⁺ (2,8)

O (2,6) gains 2 electrons → O²⁻ (2,8)

Both ions achieve stable octets. The 2 electrons from Mg are transferred to O.

Mg²⁺ [2,8] and O²⁻ [2,8]

4. Why does magnesium oxide have a higher melting point than sodium chloride?

Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ are doubly charged ions (2+ and 2-), while Na⁺ and Cl⁻ are singly charged (1+ and 1-).

The electrostatic attraction between Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ is stronger because:

  • Higher charge = stronger attraction
  • Mg²⁺ is smaller than Na⁺, so ions are closer

Therefore, more energy is needed to break the ionic bonds in MgO.

MgO: 2852°C vs NaCl: 801°C

5. Write the formulae for: (a) iron(III) oxide, (b) copper(II) sulphate, (c) ammonium nitrate

(a) Iron(III) oxide: Fe³⁺ + O²⁻ → Fe₂O₃

(b) Copper(II) sulphate: Cu²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → CuSO₄

(c) Ammonium nitrate: NH₄⁺ + NO₃⁻ → NH₄NO₃

Real-World Applications

Table Salt (NaCl)

Essential for human health, used in food preservation, and as a raw material in the chemical industry.

Calcium Carbonate (CaCO₃)

Found in limestone, marble, and chalk. Used in construction, agriculture, and as an antacid.

Sodium Fluoride (NaF)

Added to toothpaste and drinking water to prevent tooth decay.

Ammonium Nitrate (NH₄NO₃)

Important fertilizer providing nitrogen to plants. Also used in explosives.
