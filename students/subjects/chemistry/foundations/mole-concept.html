The Mole Concept & Stoichiometry

Understanding moles, molar mass, Avogadro's number, and stoichiometric calculations.

Learning Objectives

  • • Define the mole and state Avogadro's number
  • • Calculate molar mass of elements and compounds
  • • Convert between moles, mass, and number of particles
  • • Apply stoichiometry to calculate reacting masses
  • • Calculate percentage composition and empirical formulae

1 What is a Mole?

The mole is the SI unit for amount of substance. Just as we count eggs in dozens (12) or paper in reams (500), chemists count particles in moles.

One mole contains exactly:

6.02 × 10²³

particles (atoms, molecules, ions, or electrons)

This number is called Avogadro's constant (L or NA)

Why 6.02 × 10²³?

This number was chosen so that one mole of any element has a mass in grams equal to its relative atomic mass. For example:

  • • 1 mole of carbon-12 atoms = 12 g
  • • 1 mole of hydrogen atoms ≈ 1 g
  • • 1 mole of oxygen atoms ≈ 16 g

2 Molar Mass

The molar mass (M) is the mass of one mole of a substance, expressed in grams per mole (g/mol).

Elements

Molar mass = Relative atomic mass (Aᵣ) in g/mol

M(Na) = 23 g/mol

M(C) = 12 g/mol

M(Ca) = 40 g/mol

Compounds

Molar mass = Sum of atomic masses

M(H₂O) = (2×1) + 16 = 18 g/mol

M(NaCl) = 23 + 35.5 = 58.5 g/mol

M(H₂SO₄) = 2+32+64 = 98 g/mol

3 The Mole Triangle

n = m/M

moles = mass ÷ molar mass

m = n × M

mass = moles × molar mass

M = m/n

molar mass = mass ÷ moles

4 Moles and Particles

Number of particles = n × L

where L = 6.02 × 10²³

n = Particles ÷ L

to find moles from particles

Calculate the number of atoms in 4 g of hydrogen gas (H₂).

Step 1: Find molar mass of H₂

M(H₂) = 2 × 1 = 2 g/mol

Step 2: Calculate moles of H₂

n(H₂) = 4 g ÷ 2 g/mol = 2 mol

Step 3: Calculate H₂ molecules

Molecules = 2 × 6.02 × 10²³ = 1.204 × 10²⁴

Step 4: Calculate H atoms

Each H₂ molecule has 2 H atoms

Atoms = 1.204 × 10²⁴ × 2 = 2.408 × 10²⁴ atoms

5 Stoichiometry: Reacting Masses

Stoichiometry is the calculation of quantities of reactants and products in a chemical reaction. The mole ratios from balanced equations allow us to convert between substances.

Steps for Stoichiometric Calculations:

  1. 1Write the balanced chemical equation
  2. 2Identify the mole ratio from the equation
  3. 3Convert given quantity to moles
  4. 4Use mole ratio to find moles of unknown
  5. 5Convert moles to required quantity

Calculate the mass of CO₂ produced when 6 g of carbon burns completely.

Step 1: Balanced equation

C + O₂ → CO₂

Step 2: Mole ratio: 1 mol C : 1 mol CO₂

Step 3: Moles of C

n(C) = 6 ÷ 12 = 0.5 mol

Step 4: Moles of CO₂ = 0.5 mol (same ratio)

Step 5: Mass of CO₂

M(CO₂) = 12 + 32 = 44 g/mol

m(CO₂) = 0.5 × 44 = 22 g

What mass of magnesium oxide forms when 12 g of magnesium burns?

Step 1: Balanced equation

2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO

Step 2: Mole ratio: 2 mol Mg : 2 mol MgO = 1:1

Step 3: Moles of Mg

n(Mg) = 12 ÷ 24 = 0.5 mol

Step 4: Moles of MgO = 0.5 mol

Step 5: Mass of MgO

M(MgO) = 24 + 16 = 40 g/mol

m(MgO) = 0.5 × 40 = 20 g

6 Percentage Composition

The percentage composition shows what percentage of the total mass of a compound is contributed by each element.

% by mass = (mass of element / Mᵣ of compound) × 100

Calculate the % carbon in glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆).

Step 1: Find Mᵣ

Mᵣ(C₆H₁₂O₆) = (6×12) + (12×1) + (6×16) = 180

Step 2: Mass of carbon

Mass of C = 6 × 12 = 72

Step 3: Calculate %

%C = (72/180) × 100 = 40%

7 Empirical and Molecular Formulae

Empirical Formula

The simplest whole number ratio of atoms.

Example: CH₂O (for glucose)

Molecular Formula

The actual number of atoms in a molecule.

Example: C₆H₁₂O₆ (for glucose)

A compound has empirical formula CH₂O and Mᵣ = 180. Find its molecular formula.

Step 1: Empirical formula mass

= 12 + (2×1) + 16 = 30

Step 2: Find n

n = Mᵣ ÷ empirical mass = 180 ÷ 30 = 6

Step 3: Multiply empirical formula

Molecular formula = (CH₂O)₆ = C₆H₁₂O₆

Practice Problems

1. How many moles are in 88 g of CO₂?

Show Solution

M(CO₂) = 12 + 32 = 44 g/mol

n = 88 ÷ 44 = 2 mol

2. Calculate the number of molecules in 0.5 mol of water.

Show Solution

Molecules = n × L = 0.5 × 6.02 × 10²³

= 3.01 × 10²³ molecules

3. What mass of CaCO₃ contains 0.2 mol?

Show Solution

M(CaCO₃) = 40 + 12 + 48 = 100 g/mol

m = 0.2 × 100 = 20 g

4. Fe + Cl₂ → FeCl₃. What mass of FeCl₃ forms from 5.6 g of Fe?

Show Solution

Balanced: 2Fe + 3Cl₂ → 2FeCl₃

n(Fe) = 5.6 ÷ 56 = 0.1 mol

n(FeCl₃) = 0.1 mol (ratio 1:1)

M(FeCl₃) = 56 + 106.5 = 162.5 g/mol

m(FeCl₃) = 0.1 × 162.5 = 16.25 g

Key Takeaways for WAEC/JAMB/NECO

  • • Avogadro's number: L = 6.02 × 10²³ particles/mol
  • • Mole triangle: n = m/M, m = n × M, M = m/n
  • • Always use balanced equations for stoichiometry
  • • Mole ratios come from balanced equations
  • • Empirical formula: Convert % to moles, find simplest ratio
  • • Molecular formula: (Empirical formula)ₙ where n = Mᵣ ÷ empirical mass
