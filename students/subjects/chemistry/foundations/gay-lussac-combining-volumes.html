Gay-Lussac's Law of Combining Volumes

Understanding how gases combine in simple whole-number ratios by volume at constant temperature and pressure.

Learning Objectives

  • • State Gay-Lussac's Law of Combining Volumes
  • • Explain the relationship between gas volumes in reactions
  • • Apply the law to calculate reacting gas volumes
  • • Understand the connection to Avogadro's Law
  • • Solve problems involving gas volume ratios

1 The Law

Gay-Lussac's Law of Combining Volumes (1808)

"When gases react, they do so in volumes which bear a simple whole number ratio to one another and to the volumes of the products (if gaseous), provided all measurements are made at the same temperature and pressure."

Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac

French chemist who discovered this law in 1808 through careful experiments on reacting gases.

Conditions

The law applies only when all gas volumes are measured at the same temperature and pressure.

2 Key Examples

1 Hydrogen + Oxygen → Water Vapor

2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O(g)

Volume ratio:

2 : 1 : 2

(Simple whole number ratio!)

2 Nitrogen + Hydrogen → Ammonia

N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃

Volume ratio:

1 : 3 : 2

(Simple whole number ratio!)

3 Hydrogen + Chlorine → Hydrogen Chloride

H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl

Volume ratio:

1 : 1 : 2

(Simple whole number ratio!)

3 Connection to Avogadro's Law

Gay-Lussac's Law led Avogadro to propose his famous hypothesis in 1811:

Avogadro's Law

"Equal volumes of all gases, under the same conditions of temperature and pressure, contain the same number of molecules."

How They Connect:

  • Volume ratio = Mole ratio (for gases)
  • Equal volumes → Equal molecules → Equal moles
  • The coefficients in balanced equations give the volume ratios for gases

Important Note

Gay-Lussac's Law only applies to gases. Solids and liquids do not follow this law because their volumes depend on intermolecular forces and packing.

4 Worked Examples

What volume of oxygen reacts with 40 cm³ of hydrogen? What volume of steam is produced?

Equation: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O(g)

Volume ratio: 2H₂ : 1O₂ : 2H₂O = 2 : 1 : 2

Given: H₂ = 40 cm³

O₂ = (1/2) × 40 = 20 cm³

Steam produced:

H₂O = (2/2) × 40 = 40 cm³

30 cm³ of nitrogen reacts with hydrogen. Calculate the volume of hydrogen needed and ammonia produced.

Equation: N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃

Volume ratio: 1N₂ : 3H₂ : 2NH₃

Given: N₂ = 30 cm³

H₂ = (3/1) × 30 = 90 cm³

Ammonia produced:

NH₃ = (2/1) × 30 = 60 cm³

50 cm³ of HCl gas is formed from H₂ and Cl₂. What volumes of H₂ and Cl₂ were used?

Equation: H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl

Volume ratio: 1H₂ : 1Cl₂ : 2HCl

Given: HCl = 50 cm³

H₂ = (1/2) × 50 = 25 cm³

Cl₂ = (1/2) × 50 = 25 cm³

Practice Problems

1. 100 cm³ of hydrogen reacts with oxygen. Calculate the volume of oxygen needed and steam produced.

Show Solution

Equation: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

Ratio: 2 : 1 : 2

O₂ = (1/2) × 100 = 50 cm³

H₂O = (2/2) × 100 = 100 cm³

2. 60 cm³ of ammonia decomposes: 2NH₃ → N₂ + 3H₂. What volumes of N₂ and H₂ are formed?

Show Solution

Equation: 2NH₃ → N₂ + 3H₂

Ratio: 2 : 1 : 3

N₂ = (1/2) × 60 = 30 cm³

H₂ = (3/2) × 60 = 90 cm³

3. State Gay-Lussac's Law and give one example.

Show Solution

Law: When gases react, they do so in volumes that bear a simple whole number ratio to each other and to the products (if gaseous), at constant temperature and pressure.

Example: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O (volume ratio 2:1:2)

4. Why does Gay-Lussac's Law only apply to gases?

Show Solution

Because only gases have volumes that are directly proportional to the number of molecules (Avogadro's Law). Solids and liquids have volumes that depend on molecular packing and intermolecular forces, not just the number of particles.

Key Takeaways for WAEC/JAMB/NECO

  • • Gay-Lussac's Law: Gases react in simple whole number volume ratios
  • • Volume ratios = Mole ratios (for gases only)
  • • Conditions: Same temperature and pressure
  • • Connected to Avogadro's Law (equal volumes = equal molecules)
  • • The coefficients in balanced equations give volume ratios
  • • Only applies to gases, not solids or liquids
