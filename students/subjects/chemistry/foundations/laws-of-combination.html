Laws of Chemical Combination

The fundamental laws governing how elements combine to form compounds.

Learning Objectives

  • • State and explain the Law of Conservation of Mass
  • • State and explain the Law of Constant Composition
  • • State the Law of Multiple Proportions
  • • Apply these laws to solve problems

1 Introduction

When elements react to form compounds, they follow certain fundamental laws. These Laws of Chemical Combination form the basis of quantitative chemistry and were discovered through careful experiments by early chemists.

These laws helped establish Dalton's Atomic Theory and our modern understanding of atoms and molecules.

2 Law of Conservation of Mass

"Mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction."

— Antoine Lavoisier (1774)

Statement

In any chemical reaction, the total mass of the reactants equals the total mass of the products.

Implication

Atoms are neither created nor destroyed; they simply rearrange to form new substances.

When 4 g of hydrogen reacts with 32 g of oxygen, what mass of water is formed?

Apply the law: Mass of reactants = Mass of products

Calculate:

Mass of H₂O = Mass of H₂ + Mass of O₂

Mass of H₂O = 4 g + 32 g = 36 g

3 Law of Constant Composition

"A given compound always contains the same elements in the same proportion by mass."

— Joseph Proust (1799)

Also Known As:

  • • Law of Definite Proportions
  • • Law of Constant Composition

Example

Water (H₂O) always contains hydrogen and oxygen in the ratio 1:8 by mass (or 11.1% H and 88.9% O by mass), whether it comes from a river, a glacier, or a laboratory.

If 9 g of water is decomposed, what masses of hydrogen and oxygen are obtained?

Known ratio: H₂O contains H:O = 1:8 by mass

Calculate:

Total parts = 1 + 8 = 9 parts

Mass of H = (1/9) × 9 g = 1 g

Mass of O = (8/9) × 9 g = 8 g

4 Law of Multiple Proportions

"When two elements combine to form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are in simple whole number ratios."

— John Dalton (1803)

Example: Carbon and Oxygen

Compound Carbon (g) Oxygen (g)
CO (carbon monoxide)1216
CO₂ (carbon dioxide)1232

The ratio of oxygen in CO and CO₂ = 16:32 = 1:2 (simple whole numbers!)

Nitrogen forms two oxides. In NO, 14 g N combines with 16 g O. In NO₂, 14 g N combines with 32 g O. Show this illustrates the Law of Multiple Proportions.

Fixed mass of N: 14 g in both compounds

Masses of O: 16 g in NO, 32 g in NO₂

Ratio: 16:32 = 1:2

Simple whole number ratio = Law of Multiple Proportions verified!

5 Summary of Laws

Law Scientist Statement
Conservation of Mass Lavoisier Mass is neither created nor destroyed
Constant Composition Proust Compounds have fixed composition by mass
Multiple Proportions Dalton Masses combine in simple whole number ratios

Practice Problems

1. 10 g of CaCO₃ on heating gives 5.6 g of CaO and 4.4 g of CO₂. Show this illustrates the Law of Conservation of Mass.

Show Solution

Mass of reactant = 10 g

Mass of products = 5.6 + 4.4 = 10 g

Mass of reactant = Mass of products ✓

2. CuO always contains 79.9% Cu and 20.1% O by mass. Which law does this illustrate?

Show Solution

Law of Constant Composition - the compound CuO always has the same composition by mass.

3. In CO, 12 g C combines with 16 g O. In CO₂, 12 g C combines with 32 g O. What is the ratio of oxygen masses?

Show Solution

Ratio = 16:32 = 1:2

(Simple whole number ratio - Law of Multiple Proportions)
