Separation Techniques

Methods for separating mixtures and purifying substances in the laboratory.

Learning Objectives

  • • Identify appropriate separation methods for different mixtures
  • • Describe filtration, distillation, evaporation, and crystallization
  • • Explain chromatography and its applications
  • • Understand fractional distillation and separating funnels
  • • Apply separation techniques to solve practical problems

1 Why Separate Mixtures?

Mixtures can be separated because the components have different physical properties (size, boiling point, solubility, magnetism, etc.). The choice of method depends on the nature of the mixture.

Solid-Solid Mixtures

Hand picking, sieving, magnetic separation, sublimation

Solid-Liquid Mixtures

Filtration, evaporation, crystallization, centrifugation

Liquid-Liquid Mixtures

Separating funnel, fractional distillation

Gas-Gas Mixtures

Fractional distillation of liquid air

2 Filtration

Principle

Separates an insoluble solid from a liquid using a filter that allows only the liquid to pass through.

Apparatus

  • • Filter paper (folded into cone)
  • • Filter funnel
  • • Beaker/conical flask
  • • Glass rod (for pouring)

Terms

  • • Residue: Solid left on filter paper
  • • Filtrate: Liquid that passes through

Example

Separating sand from water: Sand (residue) stays on filter paper, water (filtrate) passes through.

3 Distillation

Principle

Separates a liquid from a solution by boiling, then condensing the vapor back to liquid.

Simple Distillation

For separating a liquid from a dissolved solid (different boiling points).

Example: Obtaining pure water from salt solution

Fractional Distillation

For separating miscible liquids with close boiling points.

Example: Crude oil refining, liquid air separation

Apparatus

  • • Round-bottom flask
  • • Fractionating column
  • • Condenser
  • • Receiver

Ethanol boils at 78°C, water at 100°C. How do you separate them?

Step 1: Heat the mixture in a flask with a fractionating column

Step 2: Ethanol (lower boiling point) vaporizes first at 78°C

Step 3: Vapor passes through condenser and collects as pure ethanol

Step 4: Water remains in the flask (collected later)

4 Evaporation & Crystallization

Evaporation

Used to recover a dissolved solid from a solution by heating to evaporate the solvent.

Example: Obtaining salt from salt solution

Note: Best for solids that don't decompose on heating

Crystallization

Used for solids that decompose on heating. Solution is concentrated, then cooled slowly.

Example: Obtaining copper(II) sulfate crystals

Slower cooling = larger, purer crystals

Crystallization Steps:

  1. 1. Heat solution to concentrate (don't dry completely)
  2. 2. Allow to cool slowly
  3. 3. Crystals form as solubility decreases
  4. 4. Filter to collect crystals
  5. 5. Dry between filter papers

5 Chromatography

Principle

Separates substances based on their different solubilities in a solvent and their attraction to the paper/stationary phase.

Paper Chromatography Procedure:

  1. 1 Draw a pencil line near bottom of paper
  2. 2 Place spot of mixture on the line
  3. 3 Dip paper in solvent (below the spot)
  4. 4 Solvent rises, carrying components at different speeds
  5. 5 Different components separate as spots at different heights

Rf Value

Rf = Distance traveled by substance / Distance traveled by solvent

Rf values identify substances (same compound = same Rf under same conditions)

6 Other Separation Methods

Magnetic Separation

Separates magnetic materials from non-magnetic.

Example: Iron from sulfur

Separating Funnel

Separates immiscible liquids (different densities).

Example: Oil and water

Sublimation

Separates substances that sublime from those that don't.

Example: Iodine, ammonium chloride

Centrifugation

Spins mixtures at high speed; denser particles settle.

Example: Separating blood cells from plasma

Practice Problems

1. How would you separate sand from salt?

Show Solution
  1. Add water to dissolve salt
  2. Filter to remove sand (residue)
  3. Evaporate filtrate to obtain salt

2. Which method separates: (a) Oil and water, (b) Ink colors, (c) Salt from seawater?

Show Solution

(a) Separating funnel - immiscible liquids

(b) Chromatography - separates dyes

(c) Distillation or Evaporation

3. Why is crystallization used instead of evaporation for copper(II) sulfate?

Show Solution

Copper(II) sulfate decomposes on strong heating. Crystallization allows recovery without decomposition, and produces pure crystals.

4. A substance travels 4 cm while the solvent travels 10 cm. What is its Rf value?

Show Solution

Rf = 4/10 = 0.4

Key Takeaways for WAEC/JAMB/NECO

  • • Filtration: Insoluble solid from liquid (residue + filtrate)
  • • Distillation: Liquid from solution (based on boiling points)
  • • Fractional distillation: Miscible liquids with different boiling points
  • • Evaporation: Recover dissolved solid (if heat-stable)
  • • Crystallization: For solids that decompose on heating
  • • Chromatography: Separates based on solubility (Rf values)
  • • Separating funnel: Immiscible liquids (different densities)
