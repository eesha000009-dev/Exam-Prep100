The Long Vowel Sounds

Revisiting Long Vowels

Long vowels are a cornerstone of clear English pronunciation. Unlike short vowels, their sound is elongated and often involves a change in mouth shape. This section is for dedicated practice to master their production.

  • /iː/ as in "fleece", "see", "machine"
  • /ɑː/ as in "start", "father", "calm"
  • /ɔː/ as in "thought", "law", "north"
  • /uː/ as in "goose", "two", "blue"
  • /ɜː/ as in "nurse", "stir", "learn"

Worked Example: Minimal Pairs

Distinguishing long vowels from their short counterparts is crucial. Notice the difference in length and quality:

  • /ɪ/ (short) vs. /iː/ (long): "ship" vs. "sheep"
  • /æ/ (short) vs. /ɑː/ (long): "cat" vs. "cart"
  • /ɒ/ (short) vs. /ɔː/ (long): "cot" vs. "caught"
  • /ʊ/ (short) vs. /uː/ (long): "full" vs. "fool"

Practice Drills

1. Record yourself reading these words aloud. Listen back to check the vowel length.

"seat", "feet", "reach", "mean", "believe"

2. Read this sentence, paying close attention to the long /ɔː/ sound.

"Paul saw the tall author walk across the lawn."

3. Distinguish between the vowels in these pairs:

"bid" vs. "bird", "shot" vs. "short", "had" vs. "hard"

Brief Solutions & Notes

1. All words contain the long /iː/ sound. Ensure you are not shortening it to /ɪ/ (as in "sit").

2. "Paul", "saw", "tall", "author", "walk", "lawn" all contain the long /ɔː/ sound. The mouth should be more open and rounded than for /ɒ/ (in "hot").

3. The pairs contrast a short vowel with the long /ɜː/, /ɔː/, and /ɑː/ sounds respectively. The second word in each pair should have a noticeably longer vowel sound.
