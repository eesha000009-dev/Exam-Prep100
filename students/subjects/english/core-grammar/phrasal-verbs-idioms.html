Phrasal Verbs & Idioms

Master the figurative expressions that make English natural and expressive. These are frequently tested in WAEC English Language examinations and are essential for fluent communication.

📚 Learning Objectives

  • Understand what phrasal verbs and idioms are
  • Learn common phrasal verbs with their meanings
  • Master frequently used idioms and their origins
  • Apply these expressions correctly in sentences

1 Phrasal Verbs

What is a Phrasal Verb?

A phrasal verb is a combination of a verb + particle (preposition or adverb) that creates a meaning different from the original verb. The meaning is often idiomatic—you cannot guess it from the individual words.

Example: "look up"

Literal meaning: look in an upward direction

"She looked up at the sky."

Phrasal meaning: search for information

"I will look up the word in the dictionary."

Types of Phrasal Verbs

Separable Phrasal Verbs

The object can come between the verb and particle

Turn off the light. OR Turn the light off.

Take off your shoes. OR Take your shoes off.

Note: With pronouns, separation is required: "Turn it off." (NOT "Turn off it.")

Inseparable Phrasal Verbs

The object must come after the particle

Look after the children. (NOT "Look the children after.")

Run into an old friend. (NOT "Run an old friend into.")

Intransitive Phrasal Verbs

No object follows

The car broke down.

Please sit down.

Phrasal Verb Meaning Example

2 Idioms

What is an Idiom?

An idiom is a phrase or expression whose meaning cannot be understood from the literal meaning of its individual words. Idioms are culturally specific and must be learned as whole units.

Example: "It's raining cats and dogs"

Literal meaning: Animals falling from the sky (impossible!)

Idiomatic meaning: Raining very heavily

💡 Why Learn Idioms?

  • They make your English sound more natural and native-like
  • They are frequently tested in WAEC exams
  • They help you understand native speakers and literature
  • They add color and interest to your writing

📖 Common Idioms (WAEC Essential)

Idiom Meaning Example
a piece of cake very easy The exam was a piece of cake.
break the ice start a conversation in a social setting He told a joke to break the ice.
burn the midnight oil work or study late into the night She had to burn the midnight oil to finish her project.
cost an arm and a leg very expensive That car cost an arm and a leg.
crocodile tears insincere tears; fake sadness Don't be fooled by her crocodile tears.
eat one's words admit one was wrong He had to eat his words when he lost the bet.
fish out of water uncomfortable in a new environment I felt like a fish out of water at the party.
hit the nail on the head be exactly right You hit the nail on the head with your analysis.
kill two birds with one stone achieve two things at once By cycling to work, I kill two birds with one stone—I exercise and save money.
let the cat out of the bag reveal a secret Don't let the cat out of the bag about the surprise party.
once in a blue moon very rarely We only see our relatives once in a blue moon.
see eye to eye agree completely We don't always see eye to eye on politics.
spill the beans reveal secret information Who spilled the beans about the surprise?
under the weather feeling sick or unwell I'm feeling a bit under the weather today.
when pigs fly something that will never happen He'll apologize when pigs fly.

📝 WAEC-Style Worked Examples

Example 1: Phrasal Verb Meaning

Question: Choose the meaning of the underlined phrasal verb:

"The meeting was called off due to bad weather."

A. started B. postponed C. cancelled D. delayed

Solution: C. cancelled
"Call off" means to cancel something that has been planned.

Example 2: Idiom Meaning

Question: Choose the meaning of the underlined idiom:

"The new smartphone cost an arm and a leg."

A. was damaged B. was very expensive C. was stolen D. was cheap

Solution: B. was very expensive
"Cost an arm and a leg" is an idiom meaning something is very expensive.

Example 3: Using Phrasal Verbs

Question: Replace the underlined word with a suitable phrasal verb:

"She resembles her grandmother."

A. looks after B. takes after C. comes after D. goes after

Solution: B. takes after
"Take after" means to resemble a family member in appearance or character.

Example 4: Contextual Meaning

Question: Choose the meaning of the underlined expression:

"The students broke the ice by introducing themselves."

A. broke something B. started a conversation C. caused trouble D. felt cold

Solution: B. started a conversation
"Break the ice" means to do or say something to relieve tension and start a conversation.

🔑 Key Takeaways

🔄 Phrasal Verbs

  • • Verb + particle combinations
  • • Meanings often not literal
  • • Some can be separated, some cannot
  • • Must be memorized as units

💡 Idioms

  • • Fixed expressions with figurative meaning
  • • Cannot translate literally
  • • Culturally specific
  • • Add color to language

💡 Exam Tip: In WAEC, the meaning of phrasal verbs and idioms is usually NOT literal. Always consider the figurative meaning based on context!
