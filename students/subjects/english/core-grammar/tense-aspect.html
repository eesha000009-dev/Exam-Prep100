Tense & Aspect

Master the twelve English tenses and their aspects to express time relationships accurately in your writing and speech. This is a crucial topic in WAEC English Language examinations.

📚 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the difference between tense (time) and aspect (nature of action)
  • Learn all 12 English tenses with their forms and uses
  • Identify and correct common tense errors
  • Apply correct tenses in WAEC-style questions

1 Tense vs Aspect

⏰ Tense

Indicates when an action happens - past, present, or future.

Example: "She went" (past) vs "She goes" (present)

🔄 Aspect

Indicates how an action occurs - simple, continuous, perfect, or perfect continuous.

Example: "She wrote" (simple) vs "She was writing" (continuous)

💡 Key Concept

Combining 3 tenses × 4 aspects gives us 12 English tenses. Mastering these will significantly improve your grammar accuracy in WAEC exams.

2 Present Tenses

Present Simple

Form: Base verb (add s/es for third person singular)

Examples:

  • I study English every day.
  • She teaches at the secondary school.
  • The sun rises in the east.

Uses: Habits, facts, general truths, scheduled events

Present Continuous

Form: am/is/are + verb-ing

Examples:

  • She is reading a newspaper now.
  • They are playing football outside.
  • I am writing an essay.

Uses: Actions happening now, temporary situations, future plans

Present Perfect

Form: have/has + past participle

Examples:

  • I have finished my homework.
  • She has visited Ghana twice.
  • They have lived here for ten years.

Uses: Completed actions with present relevance, experiences, actions starting in past and continuing

Present Perfect Continuous

Form: have/has been + verb-ing

Examples:

  • She has been studying for three hours.
  • They have been waiting since morning.
  • It has been raining all week.

Uses: Actions continuing from past to present, emphasizing duration

3 Past Tenses

Past Simple

Form: Verb + ed (regular) or irregular form

Examples:

  • I visited my grandmother yesterday.
  • She wrote a letter last week.
  • Nigeria gained independence in 1960.

Uses: Completed actions at a specific time in the past

Past Continuous

Form: was/were + verb-ing

Examples:

  • She was cooking when I arrived.
  • They were playing football at 5 PM.
  • While he was reading, she was writing.

Uses: Actions in progress at a specific past time, interrupted actions

Past Perfect

Form: had + past participle

Examples:

  • She had left before I arrived.
  • By the time the police came, the thief had escaped.
  • I realized I had forgotten my keys.

Uses: Actions completed before another past action (the "past of the past")

Past Perfect Continuous

Form: had been + verb-ing

Examples:

  • She had been waiting for two hours when he finally came.
  • They had been living in Lagos before they moved to Abuja.

Uses: Actions continuing up to a point in the past

4 Future Tenses

Future Simple

Form: will + base verb

Examples:

  • I will visit my parents next weekend.
  • She will be eighteen next month.
  • The examination will begin on Monday.

Uses: Predictions, promises, decisions made at the moment, future facts

Future Continuous

Form: will be + verb-ing

Examples:

  • This time tomorrow, I will be traveling to Kano.
  • She will be sleeping when you arrive.

Uses: Actions in progress at a specific future time

Future Perfect

Form: will have + past participle

Examples:

  • By next year, I will have graduated.
  • She will have finished the project by Friday.

Uses: Actions completed before a specific future time

Future Perfect Continuous

Form: will have been + verb-ing

Examples:

  • By December, I will have been studying English for five years.
  • She will have been working here for ten years next month.

Uses: Duration of an action up to a specific future time

📝 WAEC-Style Worked Examples

Example 1: Present vs Past

Question: Fill in the blank with the correct tense:

"She ______ (study) English for three years now."

Solution: "She has been studying English for three years now."
The phrase "for three years now" indicates an action that started in the past and continues to the present, requiring Present Perfect Continuous.

Example 2: Past Perfect

Question: Choose the correct verb form:

"By the time we arrived, the train ______ (leave)."

A. left B. had left C. has left D. was leaving

Solution: B. had left
The Past Perfect is needed because the train left BEFORE we arrived. Both are in the past, but one happened before the other.

Example 3: Future Perfect

Question: Rewrite using the Future Perfect tense:

"She completes the project by next week."

Solution: "She will have completed the project by next week."
"By next week" indicates completion before a future point, requiring Future Perfect.

⚠️ Common Tense Errors

❌ Incorrect: "I am understanding the lesson."

✓ Correct: "I understand the lesson."

Stative verbs (understand, know, believe, like) are rarely used in continuous tenses.

❌ Incorrect: "She has visited Lagos yesterday."

✓ Correct: "She visited Lagos yesterday."

Specific past time expressions (yesterday, last week) require Past Simple, not Present Perfect.

❌ Incorrect: "If I will see him, I will tell him."

✓ Correct: "If I see him, I will tell him."

In time clauses (if, when, after, before), use Present Simple for future meaning.

🔑 Key Takeaways

Present Tenses

Use for current habits, facts, ongoing actions, and experiences with present relevance.

Past Tenses

Use for completed actions. Past Perfect shows the "past of the past."

Future Tenses

Use for predictions, plans, and actions completed before a future point.

Time Expressions

Keywords like "since," "for," "yesterday," "by the time" signal which tense to use.

💡 Exam Tip: Look for time markers in WAEC questions. Words like "yesterday," "for five years," "by next week," and "now" are clues to the correct tense.
