Concord (Subject-Verb Agreement) & Verbals

Master the rules of subject-verb agreement and understand how verbals (infinitives, gerunds, and participles) function in sentences. These are essential skills for WAEC English Language success.

📚 Learning Objectives

  • Apply rules of subject-verb agreement in various sentence structures
  • Handle tricky agreement cases with collective nouns, indefinite pronouns, and compound subjects
  • Identify and use infinitives, gerunds, and participles correctly
  • Solve WAEC-style questions on concord and verbals

1 Basic Concord Rules

Concord means agreement between subjects and verbs in number (singular/plural) and person.

The Golden Rules

Singular Subject → Singular Verb

Singular verbs add 's' or 'es' in present tense

She writes everyday.

The boy runs fast.

Plural Subject → Plural Verb

Plural verbs do NOT add 's' or 'es'

They write everyday.

The boys run fast.

⚠️ Important Reminder

Unlike nouns, singular verbs END with 's' or 'es' while plural verbs do not. This is the opposite of how nouns work!

2 Tricky Concord Cases (WAEC Favorites)

Collective Nouns

Words like team, committee, family, audience, government, class, jury

Rule: Use singular verb when the group acts as one unit; use plural when members act individually.

The team is winning. (acting as one unit)

The jury are arguing among themselves. (acting individually)

The committee has made its decision. (one decision)

Indefinite Pronouns

Always Singular

each, everyone, everybody, someone, somebody, nobody, no one, anyone, anybody, either, neither, one

Everyone has arrived.

Always Plural

both, few, many, several, others

Many have tried.

Singular or Plural (depends on context)

some, all, any, none, most

Some of the cake is left. (singular - cake)

Some of the students are absent. (plural - students)

Neither...Nor / Either...Or

Rule: The verb agrees with the subject closer to it.

Neither the teacher nor the students were present. (students = plural)

Neither the students nor the teacher was present. (teacher = singular)

Either he or his friends are responsible. (friends = plural)

Compound Subjects (and, or, nor)

With AND: Plural verb (usually)

John and Mary are friends.

Exception: Bread and butter is my breakfast. (single unit)

With OR / NOR: Verb agrees with nearer subject

The manager or the assistants are coming.

When Subject and Verb are Separated

Rule: Ignore phrases between subject and verb. Find the true subject.

The box of chocolates

The box of chocolates is on the table. (box = singular)

One of the boys has taken the ball. (one = singular)

The list of names is long. (list = singular)

Sentences Beginning with There/Here

Rule: The subject comes AFTER the verb. The verb agrees with that subject.

There is a problem. (problem = singular)

There are many problems. (problems = plural)

Here come the students. (students = plural)

3 Verbals (Infinitives, Gerunds, Participles)

Verbals are verb forms that function as other parts of speech (nouns, adjectives, or adverbs).

Infinitives

Form: to + base verb (to go, to eat, to learn)

Function: Can act as noun, adjective, or adverb

As Noun: To swim is good exercise. (subject)

As Adjective: I need a book to read. (modifies book)

As Adverb: She came to help. (tells why)

Gerunds

Form: verb + ing (swimming, eating, learning)

Function: Always acts as a noun

As Subject: Swimming is my favorite hobby.

As Object: I enjoy reading.

As Complement: My hobby is painting.

After Prepositions: She is good at dancing.

Note: After verbs like enjoy, avoid, finish, mind, practice, and suggest, use gerunds, not infinitives.

Participles

Form: Present (-ing) or Past (-ed/-en)

Function: Act as adjectives or form verb tenses

Present Participle (-ing)

The crying baby needs attention. (adjective)

She is working hard. (continuous tense)

Past Participle (-ed/-en)

The broken window was repaired. (adjective)

She has written a letter. (perfect tense)

Comparison: Gerund vs Present Participle

Form Function Example
Gerund Noun Swimming is fun.
Present Participle Adjective/Verb The swimming pool is closed.

📝 WAEC-Style Worked Examples

Example 1: Indefinite Pronoun Agreement

Question: Choose the correct verb:

"Neither of the answers ______ correct."

A. are B. is C. were D. be

Solution: B. is
"Neither" is an indefinite pronoun that is always singular. The verb must agree with "neither," not "answers."

Example 2: Separated Subject

Question: Choose the correct verb:

"A bouquet of flowers ______ on the table."

A. were B. are C. was D. have been

Solution: C. was
The subject is "bouquet" (singular), not "flowers." The phrase "of flowers" is a prepositional phrase that should be ignored when determining agreement.

Example 3: Identifying Verbals

Question: Identify the function of the underlined word:

"Swimming is good exercise."

A. Present participle B. Gerund C. Infinitive D. Past participle

Solution: B. Gerund
"Swimming" functions as the subject of the sentence, which means it's acting as a noun. Therefore, it's a gerund.

Example 4: Collective Noun

Question: Choose the correct verb:

"The committee ______ divided on this issue."

A. is B. are C. has D. was

Solution: B. are
"Divided on this issue" shows the committee members are acting individually with different opinions, so a plural verb is appropriate.
