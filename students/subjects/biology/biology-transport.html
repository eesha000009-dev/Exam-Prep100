Transport & Circulation

Transport in Living Organisms

Transport is essential for moving materials within organisms. Learn about the circulatory system, transport in plants, and the mechanisms that sustain life in this comprehensive WAEC Biology topic.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Explain the need for transport systems
  • Describe the mammalian circulatory system
  • Explain blood composition and functions
  • Compare transport in plants and animals

1 Why Do Organisms Need Transport Systems?

All living organisms need to move substances from one part of their body to another. This is necessary because cells need nutrients, oxygen, and other materials to survive, and waste products must be removed.

Materials Transported

  • • Nutrients: Glucose, amino acids, fatty acids
  • • Respiratory gases: Oxygen, carbon dioxide
  • • Waste products: Urea, carbon dioxide
  • • Hormones: Chemical messengers
  • • Water and salts: For osmoregulation
  • • Heat: Temperature regulation

Factors Affecting Transport Need

  • • Size of the organism
  • • Level of metabolic activity
  • • Surface area to volume ratio
  • • Complexity of organization
  • • Environment (aquatic/terrestrial)

Key Point: Small organisms like Amoeba don't need specialized transport systems because they have a large surface area to volume ratio, allowing diffusion to meet their needs.

2 Transport in Animals - The Circulatory System

Types of Circulatory Systems

Open Circulatory System

Blood flows through open spaces (sinuses) in the body.

  • • Found in: Insects, molluscs
  • • Blood not always in vessels
  • • Low pressure system
  • • Direct contact with tissues

Closed Circulatory System

Blood flows entirely within blood vessels.

  • • Found in: Vertebrates, annelids
  • • Blood always in vessels
  • • High pressure system
  • • Efficient transport

Composition of Blood

Blood Plasma (55%)

Yellow liquid that carries:

  • • Water (90%)
  • • Proteins (fibrinogen, albumin, globulins)
  • • Nutrients (glucose, amino acids)
  • • Waste products (urea)
  • • Hormones and antibodies

Blood Cells (45%)

  • Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes):
    • - Transport oxygen (contain hemoglobin)
    • - Biconcave disc shape
    • - No nucleus (more space for hemoglobin)
  • White Blood Cells (Leucocytes):
    • - Fight infection (phagocytes & lymphocytes)
    • - Have nucleus
  • Platelets (Thrombocytes):
    • - Cell fragments for blood clotting

3 Transport in Plants

Plants have a vascular system consisting of two types of transport tissues: xylem and phloem.

Xylem

  • Transports: Water and dissolved minerals
  • Direction: Upward (roots → leaves)
  • Structure: Dead, hollow cells with lignified walls
  • Types: Vessels, tracheids, xylem fibers, xylem parenchyma
  • Mechanism: Transpiration pull, root pressure, capillary action

Phloem

  • Transports: Sugars (sucrose) and organic compounds
  • Direction: Both directions (translocation)
  • Structure: Living cells (sieve tubes, companion cells)
  • Types: Sieve tube elements, companion cells, phloem fibers, phloem parenchyma
  • Mechanism: Mass flow hypothesis (pressure flow)

Transpiration

The loss of water vapor from plant leaves through stomata. It creates a pulling force that draws water up through the xylem.

Factors Affecting Transpiration:

  • • Temperature: Higher temp → faster transpiration
  • • Humidity: Lower humidity → faster transpiration
  • • Wind: More wind → faster transpiration
  • • Light intensity: More light → stomata open → faster

Importance:

  • • Creates transpiration pull for water movement
  • • Cools the plant
  • • Helps in mineral absorption
  • • Maintains turgidity of cells

4 Worked Examples

WAEC 2021 Theory

Describe the adaptations of the mammalian heart to its function.

Solution:

  • 1. Muscular walls: The heart has thick muscular walls (myocardium) that contract to pump blood. The left ventricle has the thickest wall because it pumps blood to the entire body.
  • 2. Valves: Tricuspid, bicuspid (mitral), and semilunar valves prevent backflow of blood, ensuring one-way flow.
  • 3. Septum: Divides the heart into left and right sides, preventing mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood.
  • 4. Four chambers: Two atria receive blood, two ventricles pump blood out, allowing efficient double circulation.
  • 5. Coronary vessels: Supply the heart muscle with oxygen and nutrients for continuous pumping.
