1 Basic Properties of Cells

All living cells share certain properties that distinguish them from non-living matter. These properties are essential for life.

Life Processes (MRS GREN)

  • Movement - Cells can move substances
  • Respiration - Release energy from food
  • Sensitivity - Respond to stimuli
  • Growth - Increase in size/number
  • Reproduction - Produce offspring
  • Excretion - Remove waste products
  • Nutrition - Obtain and use nutrients

Cell Theory

  1. All living things are made of cells
  2. The cell is the basic unit of life
  3. All cells come from pre-existing cells

Proposed by Schleiden, Schwann, and Virchow

2 Transport Across Cell Membrane

The cell membrane is selectively permeable - it controls what enters and leaves the cell. There are three main types of transport:

1. Diffusion

The movement of particles from a region of higher concentration to lower concentration. Passive process (no energy required).

Examples: Oxygen entering cells, CO₂ leaving cells, perfume spreading in a room

Factors affecting rate: Concentration gradient, temperature, surface area, distance

2. Osmosis

The movement of water molecules from a region of higher water potential (dilute solution) to lower water potential (concentrated solution) through a selectively permeable membrane.

Effects on cells:

  • • Hypotonic solution: Water enters → Cell swells/bursts (animal), becomes turgid (plant)
  • • Hypertonic solution: Water leaves → Cell shrinks (crenates)
  • • Isotonic solution: No net movement → Cell stays same

3. Active Transport

The movement of particles from lower to higher concentration (against concentration gradient). Requires energy (ATP) and carrier proteins.

Examples:

  • • Root hair cells absorbing minerals from soil
  • • Intestine cells absorbing glucose
  • • Nerve cells pumping Na⁺/K⁺ ions

3 Cell Specialization

Differentiated cells are cells that have developed special structures to perform specific functions. The structure of a cell is always related to its function.

Red Blood Cell

  • Shape: Biconcave disc
  • Feature: No nucleus
  • Function: Transport oxygen
  • Adaptation: Large surface area for gas exchange

Root Hair Cell

  • Shape: Long extension
  • Feature: Thin wall, large vacuole
  • Function: Absorb water/minerals
  • Adaptation: Large surface area for absorption

Muscle Cell

  • Shape: Elongated, cylindrical
  • Feature: Many mitochondria
  • Function: Contraction & movement
  • Adaptation: Contains protein fibers for contraction

Nerve Cell (Neuron)

  • Shape: Long axon with branches
  • Feature: Myelin sheath
  • Function: Transmit impulses
  • Adaptation: Long to connect distant parts

Sperm Cell

  • Shape: Head, midpiece, tail
  • Feature: Many mitochondria in midpiece
  • Function: Fertilize egg
  • Adaptation: Tail for swimming to egg

Palisade Cell

  • Shape: Column-shaped
  • Feature: Many chloroplasts
  • Function: Photosynthesis
  • Adaptation: Packed with chloroplasts near light

4 Levels of Organization

Living organisms are organized in a hierarchy from simplest to most complex:

Cell

Basic unit

Tissue

Similar cells

Organ

Tissues together

System

Organs together

Organism

Complete being

Examples:

  • Tissue: Muscle tissue, nervous tissue, xylem tissue
  • Organ: Heart, leaf, kidney, stomach
  • System: Circulatory system, digestive system, nervous system
  • Organism: Human, tree, bacteria

Practice Questions

1. Explain why red blood cells have no nucleus.

Show Answer
Red blood cells have no nucleus to create more space for hemoglobin, the protein that carries oxygen. This allows each cell to carry more oxygen, making transport more efficient.

2. What is the difference between diffusion and active transport?

Show Answer
Diffusion: Movement from high to low concentration; no energy required; passive process.
Active transport: Movement from low to high concentration; requires ATP energy; uses carrier proteins.

3. What would happen to a red blood cell placed in pure water?

Show Answer
Water would enter the cell by osmosis (from high water potential outside to lower water potential inside). The cell would swell and eventually burst (lyse) because it has no cell wall to prevent over-expansion.

Key Takeaways

  • • Cells are the basic unit of life with properties like respiration, growth, reproduction
  • • Diffusion and osmosis are passive (no energy); Active transport requires ATP
  • • Cell structure is always related to function (specialization)
  • • Organization: Cell → Tissue → Organ → System → Organism
