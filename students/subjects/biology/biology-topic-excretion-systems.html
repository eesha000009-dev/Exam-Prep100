Excretory Systems in Animals

Understanding how animals remove metabolic waste products is essential for WAEC Biology. Learn about the structures, mechanisms, and products of excretion across different organisms.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Define excretion and explain its importance
  • Identify excretory organs and their products
  • Compare excretory systems in different organisms
  • Explain the structure and function of the mammalian kidney

1 What is Excretion?

Definition

Excretion is the process by which metabolic waste products are removed from the body of an organism. These waste products are produced during chemical reactions (metabolism) within cells and can be toxic if allowed to accumulate.

Metabolic Wastes Include:

  • • Carbon dioxide (CO₂)
  • • Water (H₂O)
  • • Urea
  • • Uric acid
  • • Ammonia
  • • Excess salts and minerals

Why Excretion is Important:

  • • Prevents toxic accumulation
  • • Maintains osmotic balance
  • • Regulates pH of body fluids
  • • Maintains homeostasis
  • • Prevents cell damage

Note: Excretion is different from egestion. Egestion is the removal of undigested food from the digestive tract (faeces), while excretion removes metabolic wastes from cells.

2 Excretory Products and Organs

Excretory Product Organ Source/Origin
Carbon dioxide (CO₂) Lungs Cellular respiration
Urea Kidneys Deamination of amino acids in liver
Uric acid Kidneys Breakdown of nucleic acids
Water & Salts Kidneys, Skin (sweat) Metabolic reactions, diet
Bile pigments Liver Breakdown of haemoglobin

Nitrogenous Waste Products

Nitrogenous wastes are produced from the breakdown of proteins and nucleic acids. Different animals excrete nitrogen in different forms:

Ammonia (NH₃)

  • • Very toxic
  • • Highly soluble in water
  • • Requires lots of water
  • • Excreted by aquatic animals

Urea

  • • Less toxic than ammonia
  • • Moderately soluble
  • • Requires less water
  • • Excreted by mammals

Uric Acid

  • • Least toxic
  • • Insoluble (paste form)
  • • Requires minimal water
  • • Excreted by birds, reptiles, insects

3 The Mammalian Kidney

Structure of the Kidney

Humans have two bean-shaped kidneys located on either side of the vertebral column, behind the abdominal cavity. Each kidney weighs about 150g and is approximately 10-12cm long.

Internal Structure of the Kidney

  • • Location: Outer dark-brown region of the kidney
  • • Contains: Bowman's capsules and convoluted tubules
  • • Function: Site of ultrafiltration where blood is filtered
  • • Appearance: Granular due to presence of glomeruli
  • • Location: Inner lighter-coloured region
  • • Contains: Loop of Henle and collecting ducts
  • • Structure: Made up of 8-18 renal pyramids
  • • Function: Site of water reabsorption and urine concentration
  • • Location: Funnel-shaped cavity in the centre
  • • Function: Collects urine from collecting ducts
  • • Connection: Leads into the ureter
  • • Role: Acts as a reservoir before urine passes to ureter

The nephron is the functional unit of the kidney. Each kidney contains about 1 million nephrons.

  • • Bowman's Capsule: Cup-shaped structure containing glomerulus
  • • Proximal Convoluted Tubule: Highly coiled, site of reabsorption
  • • Loop of Henle: U-shaped loop for water conservation
  • • Distal Convoluted Tubule: Regulates ion concentration
  • • Collecting Duct: Carries urine to pelvis, final water reabsorption

4 Formation of Urine

Urine formation involves three main processes that occur in the nephron:

1 Ultrafiltration

Occurs in the glomerulus and Bowman's capsule. Blood pressure forces small molecules through the glomerular capillary walls into the Bowman's capsule.

Filtered into capsule:

Water, glucose, amino acids, urea, salts, vitamins

Not filtered (too large):

Red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets, proteins

2 Selective Reabsorption

Useful substances are reabsorbed back into the blood from the filtrate as it passes through the tubules.

  • • Proximal tubule: All glucose, amino acids, 65% of water and salts
  • • Loop of Henle: Water and sodium chloride
  • • Distal tubule: Water, sodium ions, bicarbonate ions
  • • Collecting duct: Water (controlled by ADH hormone)

3 Tubular Secretion

Additional waste products are actively transported from blood into the tubules to be excreted.

  • • Hydrogen ions (H⁺) - for pH regulation
  • • Potassium ions (K⁺)
  • • Certain drugs and toxins
  • • Creatinine

Composition of Normal Urine

Main Components:

  • • Water: 95-96%
  • • Urea: 2%
  • • Sodium chloride: 1%
  • • Other salts: 0.5%
  • • Uric acid and creatinine: trace amounts

Characteristics:

  • • Colour: Pale yellow (due to urochrome)
  • • Odour: Slightly aromatic
  • • pH: Slightly acidic (6.0)
  • • Specific gravity: 1.010-1.030
  • • Should NOT contain: glucose, proteins, blood

6 Worked Examples

WAEC 2019 Theory Question

Explain why a person suffering from kidney failure may need dialysis treatment.

Solution:

  • 1. The kidneys filter blood to remove metabolic waste products like urea, excess salts, and water.
  • 2. In kidney failure, these waste products accumulate in the blood to toxic levels.
  • 3. Dialysis is an artificial method that performs the function of the kidneys.
  • 4. In dialysis, blood is passed through a dialysis machine with a semi-permeable membrane.
  • 5. Waste products diffuse from the blood (high concentration) into the dialysis fluid (low concentration).
  • 6. Essential substances like glucose remain in the blood due to their absence in the dialysis fluid.
  • 7. The cleaned blood is returned to the body, preventing toxic accumulation.
WAEC 2021 Theory Question

State three differences between excretion and egestion.

Solution:

Excretion Egestion
Removes metabolic waste products from cells Removes undigested food from the digestive tract
Products include urea, CO₂, excess water Product is faeces
Occurs in all living cells Occurs only in the digestive system
Materials were never part of the body Materials passed through but never entered cells

Key Terms to Remember

Deamination

Removal of amino group from amino acids in the liver

Glomerulus

Network of capillaries in Bowman's capsule

Ultrafiltration

Filtration under pressure through tiny pores

Osmoregulation

Control of water balance in the body

ADH (Antidiuretic Hormone)

Hormone that controls water reabsorption
