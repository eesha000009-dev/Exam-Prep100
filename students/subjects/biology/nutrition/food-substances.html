1 What are Nutrients?

Nutrients are chemical substances in food that provide energy, materials for growth and repair, and help regulate body processes. They are essential for life.

Macronutrients

Needed in large amounts for energy and growth:

  • • Carbohydrates - main energy source
  • • Proteins - growth and repair
  • • Fats (Lipids) - energy storage, insulation

Micronutrients

Needed in small amounts for body regulation:

  • • Vitamins - regulate body processes
  • • Minerals - build bones, blood, enzymes

Other Essential Substances

  • • Water - makes up 70% of body, solvent for reactions
  • • Fiber (Roughage) - aids digestion, prevents constipation

2 Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the body's main source of energy. They contain carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen with the formula Cx(H2O)y.

Monosaccharides

(Simple sugars)

  • • Glucose - blood sugar, product of digestion
  • • Fructose - fruit sugar, sweetest
  • • Galactose - milk sugar component

Formula: C₆H₁₂O₆

Disaccharides

(Double sugars)

  • • Sucrose - glucose + fructose (table sugar)
  • • Maltose - glucose + glucose (malt)
  • • Lactose - glucose + galactose (milk)

Formula: C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁

Polysaccharides

(Complex carbohydrates)

  • • Starch - storage in plants (rice, bread, potatoes)
  • • Glycogen - storage in animals (liver, muscles)
  • • Cellulose - plant cell walls (fiber)

Long chains of glucose

Sources of Carbohydrates:

Rice, bread, potatoes, yam, cassava, maize, cereals, sugar, honey, fruits

3 Proteins

Proteins are made of long chains of amino acids joined by peptide bonds. They contain carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen.

Structure

  • • Made of amino acids (20 different types)
  • • Essential amino acids - must be obtained from diet
  • • Non-essential amino acids - body can make them
  • • Dipeptide = 2 amino acids
  • • Polypeptide = many amino acids

Functions

  • • Growth - building new cells and tissues
  • • Repair - replacing damaged cells
  • • Enzymes - catalyze chemical reactions
  • • Hormones - chemical messengers (insulin)
  • • Antibodies - fight infections

Sources of Proteins:

Animal Sources (Complete proteins):

Meat, fish, eggs, milk, cheese

Plant Sources (Incomplete proteins):

Beans, peas, lentils, nuts, soya

4 Fats (Lipids)

Fats are made of glycerol and fatty acids. They contain carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen (less oxygen than carbohydrates).

Saturated Fats

  • • Solid at room temperature
  • • From animal sources
  • • Butter, lard, meat fat
  • • Can increase blood cholesterol
  • • Linked to heart disease

Unsaturated Fats

  • • Liquid at room temperature (oils)
  • • From plant sources
  • • Vegetable oils, fish oils, nuts
  • • Can help reduce cholesterol
  • • Better for heart health

Functions of Fats

  • • Energy storage - provide 2× more energy than carbohydrates
  • • Insulation - maintain body temperature
  • • Protection - cushion organs from damage
  • • Cell membranes - phospholipids form membranes
  • • Vitamin absorption - help absorb vitamins A, D, E, K

5 Vitamins

Vitamins are organic compounds needed in small amounts for normal metabolism. They cannot be synthesized by the body and must be obtained from the diet.

Vitamin Sources Function Deficiency
Vitamin A Liver, carrots, dark green vegetables, fish oil Making rhodopsin for vision in dim light; healthy skin Night blindness, dry skin
Vitamin B1 (Thiamine) Whole grains, yeast, meat, beans Carbohydrate metabolism; nerve function Beri-beri (weak muscles, fatigue)
Vitamin C Citrus fruits, tomatoes, peppers, green vegetables Collagen formation; wound healing; immunity Scurvy (bleeding gums, slow healing)
Vitamin D Fish oil, eggs, liver; sunlight on skin Calcium absorption; bone formation Rickets (soft bones), osteomalacia
Vitamin K Green leafy vegetables, liver Blood clotting Excessive bleeding

6 Minerals

Minerals are inorganic elements needed in small amounts for various body functions.

Mineral Sources Function Deficiency
Iron (Fe) Red meat, liver, spinach, beans Making hemoglobin in red blood cells Anemia (tiredness, pale skin)
Calcium (Ca) Milk, cheese, yogurt, green vegetables Strong bones and teeth; blood clotting; muscle contraction Rickets, osteoporosis
Iodine (I) Seafood, iodized salt Making thyroid hormones Goitre (swollen thyroid)
Phosphorus (P) Meat, fish, eggs, dairy Bones and teeth; ATP production; DNA Weak bones, fatigue

7 Food Tests

Chemical tests are used to identify the presence of different food substances.

Food Substance Test Procedure Positive Result
Starch Iodine Test Add iodine solution to food sample Blue-black
Reducing Sugars Benedict's Test Add Benedict's solution, heat in water bath Brick-red precipitate
Protein Biuret Test Add Biuret reagent (NaOH + CuSO₄) Purple/Violet
Fats (Lipids) Ethanol Emulsion Test Shake with ethanol, add water White emulsion

Practice Questions

1. Why do athletes need more carbohydrates in their diet?

Show Answer
Athletes need more carbohydrates because they are the main source of energy for the body. During exercise, the body breaks down glycogen (stored carbohydrate) to glucose, which is used for respiration to release energy. More carbohydrates ensure sufficient energy reserves for prolonged physical activity.

2. Describe the test for starch and the expected result.

Show Answer
Test: Add a few drops of iodine solution to the food sample.
Positive result: The iodine solution changes from brown/orange to blue-black color.
Negative result: The iodine solution remains brown/orange.

3. Explain why a lack of iron in the diet causes anemia.

Show Answer
Iron is essential for making hemoglobin, the red pigment in red blood cells that carries oxygen. Without enough iron, the body cannot produce enough hemoglobin or red blood cells. This means less oxygen is transported to tissues, causing symptoms like tiredness, weakness, and pale skin - a condition called anemia.

Key Takeaways

  • • Macronutrients: Carbohydrates (energy), Proteins (growth/repair), Fats (energy storage/insulation)
  • • Micronutrients: Vitamins and minerals regulate body processes
  • • Food tests: Iodine (starch), Benedict's (sugars), Biuret (protein), Ethanol emulsion (fats)
  • • Deficiency diseases: Scurvy (vitamin C), Rickets (vitamin D), Anemia (iron), Goitre (iodine)
  • • A balanced diet contains all nutrients in correct proportions
