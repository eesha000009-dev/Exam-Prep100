1 What is Homeostasis?

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a constant internal environment despite changes in external conditions.

Your body must maintain stable internal conditions for cells to function properly. This includes maintaining constant:

Temperature

~37°C (98.6°F)

Water Balance

Blood water potential

Blood Glucose

~90 mg/100mL

Why is Homeostasis Important?

  • • Enzymes work best at specific temperatures and pH levels
  • • Cells need constant water levels for metabolic reactions
  • • Glucose is needed for respiration to produce energy
  • • Changes can lead to illness or death

2 Negative Feedback Mechanism

Homeostasis is maintained through negative feedback - a control mechanism that reverses any change from the normal level.

Think of it like a thermostat!

If temperature rises above set point → cooling systems activate → temperature drops back to normal. If temperature drops below set point → heating systems activate → temperature rises back to normal.

3 Thermoregulation (Temperature Control)

The hypothalamus in the brain acts as the body's thermostat, detecting changes in blood temperature and skin receptors.

When Body is TOO HOT

  • Sweating: Sweat glands release sweat; evaporation cools skin
  • Vasodilation: Blood vessels widen; more blood to skin surface; heat lost by radiation
  • Hairs lie flat: Less air trapped, more heat loss
  • Reduced metabolism: Less heat produced

When Body is TOO COLD

  • Shivering: Rapid muscle contractions generate heat
  • Vasoconstriction: Blood vessels narrow; less blood to skin; heat conserved
  • Hairs stand up: Traps insulating air layer (goosebumps)
  • Increased metabolism: More heat produced

Dangers of Temperature Extremes

Hyperthermia (Too Hot):

Heat exhaustion, heat stroke, enzymes denature (>40°C)

Hypothermia (Too Cold):

Slowed metabolism, confusion, organ failure (<35°C)

4 Blood Glucose Regulation

The pancreas monitors blood glucose levels and releases hormones to maintain normal levels (70-110 mg/dL).

High Blood Glucose (After Eating)

β-cells in pancreas release INSULIN

  • • Liver takes up glucose, converts to glycogen
  • • Muscle cells take up glucose
  • • Fat cells store excess as fat
  • • Blood glucose level decreases

Low Blood Glucose (Fasting)

α-cells in pancreas release GLUCAGON

  • • Liver breaks down glycogen into glucose
  • • Liver produces glucose from fats/amino acids
  • • Glucose released into bloodstream
  • • Blood glucose level increases

Diabetes Mellitus

Type 1: Autoimmune - pancreas doesn't produce insulin. Requires insulin injections.

Type 2: Body cells become resistant to insulin. Managed with diet, exercise, and medication.

5 Osmoregulation (Water Balance)

The kidneys regulate water and salt balance in the blood. This is controlled by the hormone ADH (Anti-Diuretic Hormone).

How ADH Works

1

Low water in blood → Detected by hypothalamus

2

Pituitary gland releases ADH

3

ADH makes kidney collecting ducts more permeable to water

4

More water reabsorbed → Less, more concentrated urine

High Water in Blood

Less ADH released → Collecting ducts less permeable → More water excreted → Large amount of dilute urine

Practice Questions

1. What is the role of the hypothalamus in thermoregulation?

Show Answer
The hypothalamus acts as the body's thermostat. It detects changes in blood temperature through its own receptors and receives signals from skin thermoreceptors. It then coordinates responses (sweating, shivering, vasodilation/vasoconstriction) to restore normal temperature.

2. Explain why someone with Type 1 diabetes needs insulin injections.

Show Answer
In Type 1 diabetes, the immune system destroys the β-cells in the pancreas that produce insulin. Without insulin, glucose cannot enter body cells and builds up in the blood. Insulin injections replace the missing hormone, allowing cells to take up glucose.

Key Takeaways

  • • Homeostasis = maintaining constant internal environment
  • • Negative feedback reverses changes to restore normal levels
  • • Thermoregulation: Hypothalamus controls sweating, shivering, vasodilation/vasoconstriction
  • • Glucose regulation: Insulin lowers, Glucagon raises blood glucose
  • • Osmoregulation: ADH controls water reabsorption in kidneys
