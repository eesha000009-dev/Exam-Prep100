Solving Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation is any equation that can be written as ax² + bx + c = 0. "Solving" it means finding the values of 'x' that make the equation true. Let's explore the common methods.

Forming & Applying Equations

Forming an Equation from Given Roots

The Big Idea: This is factorization in reverse. If you know the roots, you can rebuild the equation.

If the roots are α and β, the equation is x² - (sum of roots)x + (product of roots) = 0.
Example: If roots are 4 and -5, the Sum = -1 and the Product = -20. The equation is x² - (-1)x + (-20) = 0, which simplifies to x² + x - 20 = 0.

Application in Practical Problems

The Big Idea: Translate a real-world word problem into a quadratic equation to solve it.

How to Translate Word Problems:

  • Identify the unknown: Call it 'x' or another variable (like 'w' for width).
  • Formulate the equation: Use the information given (e.g., area = length × width) to write an equation.
  • Solve: Use one of the methods above to find the value of your unknown.
  • Check your answer: Does the solution make sense in the real world? (e.g., length cannot be negative).

Graphs of Quadratic Functions

Visualizing quadratic equations is a powerful way to understand them. When you plot y = ax² + bx + c, you get a special U-shaped curve.

What is a Parabola?

A parabola is the name for the symmetrical, U-shaped curve created by a quadratic equation. It can open upwards (like a smile) if 'a' is positive, or downwards (like a frown) if 'a' is negative.

Drawing the Graph (Step-by-Step)

  1. Create a Table of Values: Choose several x-values and plug them into the equation to find the corresponding y-values.
  2. Plot the Points: Mark these (x, y) coordinate pairs on your graph paper.
  3. Draw the Curve: Connect the points with a smooth, continuous curve to form your parabola.

Finding Roots from the Graph

The "roots" or "solutions" are simply the x-values where the parabola intersects the x-axis (the horizontal line). Think of it as where the curve hits the ground.

Interactive Demo: Drag the points A and B to change the equation and see how the roots change. The x-coordinates where the parabola crosses the x-axis are the roots.

Key Points:

  • A parabola can cross the x-axis 0, 1, or 2 times
  • Two crossings = two real roots (most common)
  • One crossing = one repeated root
  • No crossing = no real roots (complex roots)

Solving a System of Equations Graphically

To solve a pair of equations like y = ax² + bx + c (a curve) and y = mx + k (a straight line), draw both on the same graph. The points where the line and the parabola cross are the solutions.

Interactive Demo: Drag points to modify both the parabola and the line. The intersection points (if any) are the solutions to both equations.

Possible Outcomes:

  • Two intersections = two solutions
  • One intersection = one solution (line touches the parabola)
  • No intersections = no solutions (line doesn't meet the parabola)

Finding the Gradient with a Tangent

A tangent is a straight line that "just kisses" the curve at a single point without crossing through it. The slope (or gradient) of this tangent line tells you how steep the curve is at that exact point.

Interactive Demo: Move point A along the curve to see how the tangent line changes. The slope of this line gives you the gradient of the curve at that point.

How to Find the Gradient:

  1. Draw the tangent line at your chosen point
  2. Pick two points on your tangent line (not too close together)
  3. Calculate the gradient using: rise ÷ run or (y₂-y₁)/(x₂-x₁)
