Equilibrium of Body in Liquids

Buoyancy, Archimedes' principle, upthrust, floatation and stability explained with examples.

Buoyancy & Upthrust

When a body is immersed in a fluid it experiences an upward force called upthrust or buoyant force. This is due to pressure difference between lower and upper surfaces.

  • Magnitude of upthrust = weight of fluid displaced
  • Direction: vertically upward
  • Depends on volume immersed and fluid density

Archimedes' Principle

An immersed body is buoyed up by a force equal to the weight of fluid displaced by it. Useful for measuring density and determining floatation.

Formula: Upthrust U = ρfluid × Vdisplaced × g

Equilibrium & Floatation

For a floating body, weight equals upthrust. For a submerged body in equilibrium, resultant force is zero.

  1. Floatation condition: weight = upthrust
  2. Partially submerged bodies displace fluid equal to their weight
  3. Completely submerged bodies experience net force depending on density

Stability of Floating Bodies

Stability depends on the relative positions of center of gravity (G) and center of buoyancy (B). For stable equilibrium, G must be below the metacentre (M).

  • Metacentre (M): point where buoyant force acts when body is slightly tilted
  • Stable if G below M, unstable if G above M
  • Neutral if G and M coincide

Examples & Problems

Example 1: Floating Block

A wooden block of density 600 kg/m³ floats in water (ρ=1000 kg/m³). Find fraction submerged.

Solution idea: fraction submerged = density(block)/density(fluid) = 0.6

Example 2: Determining Density

Use Archimedes' principle to find density of an irregular solid by measuring weight in air and apparent loss in water.
