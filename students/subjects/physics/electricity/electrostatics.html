Electric Charges & Electrostatics

Coulomb's law, electric field and potential — core concepts for forces between charges.

Coulomb's Law

Two point charges exert force on each other proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. The magnitude is given by F = k q1 q2 / r^2.

Electric Field

The electric field at a point is defined as the force per unit positive charge placed at that point. Field of a point charge: E = k q / r^2 (radial).

Electric Potential

Potential (V) is the work done per unit charge to bring a small positive test charge from infinity to the point. For a point charge V = k q / r.

Worked Example

Find the force between two charges of 2 μC and 3 μC separated by 0.5 m. Use k = 9×10^9 N·m²/C².

Solution sketch:

Compute F = k q1 q2 / r^2 = 9e9 × 2e-6 × 3e-6 / 0.5^2 ≈ 216 N (repulsive if same sign).

Applications & Notes

  • Superposition principle for multiple charges.
  • Fields from continuous charge distributions can be found by integration (line, surface, volume).
  • Equipotential surfaces are perpendicular to electric field lines.
