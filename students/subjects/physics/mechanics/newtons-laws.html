I Newton's First Law: The Law of Inertia

"An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, unless acted upon by an unbalanced external force."

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. The greater the mass, the greater the inertia.

Examples of Inertia

  • • Seatbelts: Your body keeps moving forward when a car stops suddenly
  • • Headrests: Protect against whiplash in rear-end collisions
  • • Table cloth trick: Dishes stay in place when cloth is pulled quickly
  • • Sports: A soccer ball keeps rolling until friction stops it

Key Points

  • • Mass is a measure of inertia
  • • Heavier objects have more inertia (harder to start or stop)
  • • No force needed to maintain motion (contrary to Aristotle!)
  • • Friction often masks the first law in everyday life

II Newton's Second Law: F = ma

"The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass."

F = ma

Force (N) = Mass (kg) × Acceleration (m/s²)

Force (F)

Measured in Newtons (N)

1 N = 1 kg·m/s²

Mass (m)

Measured in kilograms (kg)

Amount of matter

Acceleration (a)

Measured in m/s²

Rate of velocity change

Worked Example

A 5 kg box is pushed with a force of 20 N. What is its acceleration?

F = ma → a = F/m

a = 20 N ÷ 5 kg = 4 m/s²

If Force ↑ and Mass constant:

Acceleration ↑ (directly proportional)

If Mass ↑ and Force constant:

Acceleration ↓ (inversely proportional)

III Newton's Third Law: Action-Reaction

"For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

When object A exerts a force on object B, object B simultaneously exerts an equal and opposite force on object A. These forces always come in pairs!

Rocket Propulsion

Hot gases are expelled downward (action); Rocket moves upward (reaction)

Walking

You push ground backward (action); Ground pushes you forward (reaction)

Swimming

You push water backward (action); Water pushes you forward (reaction)

Book on Table

Book pushes table down (action); Table pushes book up (reaction) - Normal force!

Common Misconception

The action and reaction forces act on different objects, so they don't cancel out! That's why objects can still move. If you push a box, you push it forward while it pushes you backward - the box moves because only the forward force acts on IT.

Practice Questions

1. A 10 kg box accelerates at 2 m/s². What force is applied?

Show Answer

F = ma

F = 10 kg × 2 m/s² = 20 N

2. A force of 50 N causes an object to accelerate at 5 m/s². What is its mass?

Show Answer

F = ma → m = F/a

m = 50 N ÷ 5 m/s² = 10 kg

3. Explain why you move forward when you jump off a boat.

Show Answer
Newton's Third Law (Action-Reaction): When you push backward against the boat (action), the boat pushes you forward (reaction). This is why the boat moves backward as you jump forward.

4. Why do passengers lurch forward when a car brakes suddenly?

Show Answer
Newton's First Law (Inertia): The passengers' bodies want to keep moving at the same speed (inertia). When the car stops, there's no force directly stopping the passengers, so they continue moving forward until the seatbelt or dashboard exerts a force on them.

Key Takeaways

  • • 1st Law: Objects resist changes in motion (inertia) - need force to change velocity
  • • 2nd Law: F = ma - force causes acceleration, mass resists it
  • • 3rd Law: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction (on different objects!)
  • • Mass measures inertia - more mass = harder to accelerate
  • • Forces always come in pairs - you can't touch without being touched
