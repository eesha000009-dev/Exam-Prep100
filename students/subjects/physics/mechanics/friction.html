Friction

Understanding the force that resists relative motion between surfaces

Types of Friction

Static Friction

  • • Acts when object is stationary
  • • Maximum value: fs ≤ μsN
  • • Prevents motion initiation
  • • Variable magnitude up to maximum

Kinetic Friction

  • • Acts during motion
  • • Constant value: fk = μkN
  • • Opposes ongoing motion
  • • Generally less than static friction

Friction Coefficients

Static Coefficient (μs)

  • • Wood on wood: 0.25-0.50
  • • Metal on metal: 0.15-0.60
  • • Rubber on concrete: 0.60-0.85
  • • Ice on ice: 0.10

Kinetic Coefficient (μk)

  • • Wood on wood: 0.20
  • • Metal on metal: 0.15
  • • Rubber on concrete: 0.50
  • • Ice on ice: 0.03

Laws and Properties

Basic Laws

  • • Proportional to normal force
  • • Independent of contact area
  • • Depends on surface properties
  • • Always parallel to surface

Important Properties

  • • Direction opposes motion/tendency
  • • μs > μk (usually)
  • • Temperature dependent
  • • Surface cleanliness matters

Problem-Solving Examples

Example 1: Block on Incline

Find the minimum coefficient of static friction needed to prevent a block from sliding down a 30° incline.

Solution approach:

  1. Draw force diagram
  2. Resolve forces parallel and perpendicular to incline
  3. Apply equilibrium conditions
  4. Solve for μs

Example 2: Pulling a Block

Calculate the force needed to pull a 10kg block across a horizontal surface with μk = 0.2.

Key points:

  • Normal force = weight
  • Kinetic friction opposes motion
  • Use F = μkN
