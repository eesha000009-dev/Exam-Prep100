1 Why is Measurement Important?

Measurement is the process of determining the size, amount, or degree of something using standard units. In physics, accurate measurement is essential because:

Why Measure?

  • • To verify scientific theories
  • • To make accurate predictions
  • • To ensure safety and precision
  • • To communicate results clearly
  • • To reproduce experiments

Key Concepts

  • • Accuracy: How close to true value
  • • Precision: Consistency of measurements
  • • Error: Difference from true value
  • • Uncertainty: Range of possible values

Did You Know?

The metric system (SI units) was developed during the French Revolution (1790s) to create a universal, decimal-based system of measurement. Today, it's used by scientists worldwide!

2 Measuring Instruments

Different physical quantities require different measuring instruments. Here are the most common ones used in physics:

Instrument Measures Range Accuracy
Metre Rule Length 0 - 100 cm 0.1 cm (1 mm)
Vernier Caliper Length (internal/external) 0 - 15 cm 0.01 cm (0.1 mm)
Micrometer Screw Gauge Very small lengths 0 - 2.5 cm 0.001 cm (0.01 mm)
Stopwatch Time 0 - variable 0.01 s
Beam Balance Mass 0 - 500 g (typical) 0.01 g
Thermometer Temperature -10°C to 110°C 0.5°C or 1°C
Measuring Cylinder Volume (liquids) Various sizes Depends on size

3 How to Read Instruments Correctly

Avoiding Parallax Error

Parallax error occurs when reading an instrument from an angle rather than directly from above. Always position your eye perpendicular to the scale!

Correct technique: Position your eye directly above the marking when reading a metre rule, measuring cylinder, or any scale.

Reading the Meniscus

When reading liquid volumes, the surface curves to form a meniscus. Always read from the bottom of the meniscus for accurate measurements.

For water: The meniscus curves downward. Read from the bottom.

For mercury: The meniscus curves upward. Read from the top.

Reading a Vernier Caliper

  1. Read the main scale reading just before the zero of the vernier scale
  2. Find which vernier scale mark aligns perfectly with a main scale mark
  3. Read that vernier scale value
  4. Total = Main scale reading + Vernier scale reading

Reading a Micrometer Screw Gauge

  1. Read the sleeve (main) scale - note whole millimeters and half-millimeters
  2. Read the thimble scale - each division = 0.01 mm
  3. Total = Sleeve reading + Thimble reading

Practice Questions

1. What is the reading on a metre rule if the object starts at the 2.0 cm mark and ends at the 15.3 cm mark?

Show Answer
Length = Final reading - Initial reading = 15.3 cm - 2.0 cm = 13.3 cm

2. A student measures the length of a pencil five times: 14.2 cm, 14.4 cm, 14.1 cm, 14.3 cm, 14.2 cm. Calculate the average length.

Show Answer

Average = (14.2 + 14.4 + 14.1 + 14.3 + 14.2) ÷ 5

Average = 71.2 ÷ 5 = 14.24 cm

Taking repeated measurements and averaging reduces random errors!

3. State two precautions to take when using a measuring cylinder.

Show Answer
  1. Place on a flat, horizontal surface
  2. Read from the bottom of the meniscus
  3. Position eye perpendicular to scale (avoid parallax error)
  4. Wait for the liquid to settle before reading

4. What is the precision of a Vernier caliper?

Show Answer
The precision of a Vernier caliper is 0.01 cm (or 0.1 mm). This means it can measure to one-tenth of a millimeter.

Key Takeaways

  • • Measurement is fundamental to physics - it allows us to quantify and verify
  • • Choose the right instrument for the required precision
  • • Always read from the correct position to avoid parallax error
  • • Read the bottom of the meniscus for liquids like water
  • • Taking multiple readings and averaging reduces random errors
