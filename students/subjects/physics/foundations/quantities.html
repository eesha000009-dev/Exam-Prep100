Fundamental & Derived Quantities

Understanding the basic and complex physical quantities that describe our universe

What are Physical Quantities?

A physical quantity is any property that can be measured or calculated. Every physical quantity consists of two parts:

  • A numerical value (magnitude)
  • A unit of measurement

Fundamental (Base) Quantities

These are the basic quantities that cannot be expressed in terms of other quantities:

Quantity SI Unit Symbol
Length meter m
Mass kilogram kg
Time second s
Electric Current ampere A
Temperature kelvin K
Amount of Substance mole mol
Luminous Intensity candela cd

Derived Quantities

These quantities are expressed in terms of fundamental quantities:

Quantity Formula SI Unit Unit Formula
Area length × width square meter m²
Volume length × width × height cubic meter m³
Velocity displacement/time meter per second m/s
Acceleration velocity/time meter per second squared m/s²
Force mass × acceleration newton kg⋅m/s²
Energy force × displacement joule kg⋅m²/s²

Key Points to Remember

About Fundamental Quantities

  • • They are independent of each other
  • • They form the basis of all measurements
  • • They have unique SI units
  • • They can't be broken down further

About Derived Quantities

  • • They are combinations of fundamental quantities
  • • Their units can be expressed in terms of fundamental units
  • • They often have special names (e.g., newton, joule)
  • • They can be derived using dimensional analysis

Practice Problems

  1. Problem 1:

    Express pressure (force per unit area) in terms of fundamental quantities.

    Think: Force is kg⋅m/s², area is m². Final units will be kg/(m⋅s²)

  2. Problem 2:

    Identify whether density (mass per unit volume) is a fundamental or derived quantity. Express it in terms of fundamental units.

    Think: Consider the units of mass (kg) and volume (m³)
