1 What is Titration?

Titration is a quantitative analytical technique used to determine the concentration of an unknown solution by reacting it with a solution of known concentration (standard solution).

Key Terms

  • Titrand: Solution being analyzed (unknown concentration)
  • Titrant: Standard solution in burette (known concentration)
  • Endpoint: Point where reaction is complete (color change)
  • Equivalence Point: Stoichiometrically equivalent amounts have reacted

Key Formula

MₐVₐ = MᵦVᵦ

For 1:1 mole ratio reactions

Where M = molarity, V = volume, a = acid, b = base

2 Apparatus Used in Titration

🧪

Burette

Holds titrant, reads from 0 at top to 50 at bottom

Precision: ±0.05 cm³

🔬

Pipette

Measures fixed volume of titrand into flask

Common sizes: 25 cm³, 10 cm³

⚗️

Conical Flask

Contains titrand + indicator for reaction

Shape allows swirling

📏

Burette Stand

Holds burette vertically during titration

💧

Funnel

Used to fill burette without spillage

🎯

White Tile

Provides white background for color change

3 Titration Procedure

A. Preparation

  1. Rinse burette with distilled water, then with titrant solution
  2. Fill burette with titrant above zero mark, then drain to zero
  3. Record initial burette reading
  4. Rinse pipette with titrand solution
  5. Transfer measured volume of titrand to clean conical flask
  6. Add 2-3 drops of appropriate indicator

B. Titration

  1. Place conical flask on white tile under burette
  2. Add titrant slowly while swirling flask continuously
  3. Near endpoint, add titrant drop by drop
  4. Stop when permanent color change occurs
  5. Record final burette reading
  6. Calculate volume used (final - initial)

C. Recording Results

Repeat titration until you obtain concordant results (volumes within 0.1 cm³ of each other). Take average of concordant values.

4 Choice of Indicators

Titration Type pH Range Suitable Indicator Color Change
Strong acid + Strong base 3-11 Phenolphthalein OR Methyl orange Colorless → Pink OR Red → Yellow
Strong acid + Weak base 3-7 Methyl orange Red → Yellow (at pH ~4)
Weak acid + Strong base 7-11 Phenolphthalein Colorless → Pink (at pH ~9)
Weak acid + Weak base ~7 No suitable indicator Use pH meter instead

5 Worked Example

Problem

25.0 cm³ of 0.1 mol/dm³ HCl required 20.0 cm³ of NaOH solution for neutralization. Calculate the concentration of the NaOH solution.

Step 1: Write the balanced equation

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

Mole ratio = 1:1

Step 2: Calculate moles of HCl

Moles HCl = Concentration × Volume (dm³)

Moles HCl = 0.1 × (25.0/1000) = 0.0025 mol

Step 3: Calculate moles of NaOH

From equation: 1 mol HCl reacts with 1 mol NaOH

Moles NaOH = 0.0025 mol

Step 4: Calculate concentration of NaOH

Concentration = Moles / Volume (dm³)

Concentration = 0.0025 / (20.0/1000)

Concentration NaOH = 0.125 mol/dm³

7 Common Errors & Precautions

Common Errors

  • • Reading burette from bottom up (should be top down)
  • • Not removing funnel before titration
  • • Not rinsing apparatus with correct solutions
  • • Adding too much indicator
  • • Not swirling flask during titration
  • • Air bubbles in burette tip

Precautions

  • • Read burette at eye level (meniscus)
  • • Remove funnel before starting titration
  • • Rinse burette with titrant, pipette with titrand
  • • Use only 2-3 drops of indicator
  • • Swirl continuously while adding titrant
  • • Check for and remove air bubbles

Key Takeaways

  • • Titration determines unknown concentration using a standard solution
  • • Key formula: M₁V₁/n₁ = M₂V₂/n₂ (accounting for mole ratio)
  • • Choose indicator based on acid-base strength combination
  • • Concordant results = values within 0.1 cm³ of each other
  • • Always read burette from top (0) to bottom (50)
