The Mole Concept & Stoichiometry

Learning Objectives

  • Define the mole and Avogadro's number
  • Calculate molar mass of elements and compounds
  • Convert between moles, mass, and number of particles
  • Apply stoichiometry to calculate reacting masses
  • Calculate percentage composition and empirical formulae

What is a Mole?

The mole is the SI unit for amount of substance. Just as we count eggs in dozens (12) or paper in reams (500 sheets), chemists count particles in moles.

One mole contains exactly:

6.02 × 10²³

particles (atoms, molecules, ions, or electrons)

This number is called Avogadro's constant (L or NA)

Why 6.02 × 10²³?

This number was chosen so that one mole of any element has a mass in grams equal to its relative atomic mass. For example:

  • 1 mole of carbon-12 atoms has a mass of exactly 12 g
  • 1 mole of hydrogen atoms has a mass of approximately 1 g
  • 1 mole of oxygen atoms has a mass of approximately 16 g

Molar Mass

The molar mass (M) of a substance is the mass of one mole of that substance. It is expressed in grams per mole (g/mol).

Elements

Molar mass = Relative atomic mass (Aᵣ) in g/mol

M(Na) = 23 g/mol

M(C) = 12 g/mol

M(Ca) = 40 g/mol

Compounds

Molar mass = Sum of atomic masses in formula

M(H₂O) = (2×1) + 16 = 18 g/mol

M(NaCl) = 23 + 35.5 = 58.5 g/mol

M(H₂SO₄) = (2×1) + 32 + (4×16) = 98 g/mol

The Mole Triangle

The mole triangle helps you remember the relationships between mass, moles, and molar mass:

n = m/M

moles = mass ÷ molar mass

m = n × M

mass = moles × molar mass

M = m/n

molar mass = mass ÷ moles

Relationship Between Moles and Particles

Number of particles = n × L

where L = 6.02 × 10²³

n = Number of particles ÷ L

to find moles from particles

📝 Worked Example 1

Calculate the number of atoms in 4 g of hydrogen gas (H₂).

Solution:

Step 1: Find molar mass of H₂

M(H₂) = 2 × 1 = 2 g/mol

Step 2: Calculate moles of H₂

n(H₂) = 4 g ÷ 2 g/mol = 2 mol

Step 3: Calculate number of H₂ molecules

Molecules = 2 × 6.02 × 10²³ = 1.204 × 10²⁴ molecules

Step 4: Calculate number of H atoms

Each H₂ molecule has 2 H atoms

Atoms = 1.204 × 10²⁴ × 2 = 2.408 × 10²⁴ atoms

Stoichiometry: Calculating Reacting Masses

Stoichiometry is the calculation of the quantities of reactants and products involved in a chemical reaction. The mole ratios in a balanced equation allow us to convert between amounts of different substances.

Steps for Stoichiometric Calculations

  1. Write the balanced chemical equation
  2. Identify the mole ratio from the equation
  3. Convert given quantity to moles
  4. Use the mole ratio to find moles of unknown
  5. Convert moles of unknown to required quantity

📝 Worked Example 2

Calculate the mass of carbon dioxide produced when 6 g of carbon is completely burned in oxygen.

Solution:

Step 1: Write balanced equation

C + O₂ → CO₂

Step 2: Mole ratio

1 mol C : 1 mol CO₂

Step 3: Calculate moles of C

n(C) = 6 g ÷ 12 g/mol = 0.5 mol

Step 4: Calculate moles of CO₂ (same as C)

n(CO₂) = 0.5 mol

Step 5: Calculate mass of CO₂

M(CO₂) = 12 + (2×16) = 44 g/mol

m(CO₂) = 0.5 mol × 44 g/mol = 22 g

Answer: 22 g of CO₂ is produced

📝 Worked Example 3

What mass of magnesium oxide is formed when 12 g of magnesium burns in air?

Solution:

Step 1: Write balanced equation

2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO

Step 2: Mole ratio

2 mol Mg : 2 mol MgO (or 1:1)

Step 3: Calculate moles of Mg

n(Mg) = 12 g ÷ 24 g/mol = 0.5 mol

Step 4: Calculate moles of MgO

n(MgO) = 0.5 mol (1:1 ratio)

Step 5: Calculate mass of MgO

M(MgO) = 24 + 16 = 40 g/mol

m(MgO) = 0.5 mol × 40 g/mol = 20 g

Answer: 20 g of MgO is formed

Percentage Composition

The percentage composition shows what percentage of the total mass of a compound is contributed by each element.

% by mass of element = (mass of element in compound / Mᵣ of compound) × 100

📝 Worked Example 4

Calculate the percentage composition of carbon in glucose, C₆H₁₂O₆.

Solution:

Mᵣ(C₆H₁₂O₆) = (6×12) + (12×1) + (6×16) = 72 + 12 + 96 = 180

Mass of carbon = 6 × 12 = 72

%C = (72/180) × 100

%C = 40%

Similarly: %H = (12/180) × 100 = 6.67%

%O = (96/180) × 100 = 53.33%

Empirical and Molecular Formulae

Empirical Formula

The simplest whole number ratio of atoms of each element in a compound.

Example: CH₂O (for glucose)

Molecular Formula

The actual number of atoms of each element in a molecule.

Example: C₆H₁₂O₆ (for glucose)

📝 Worked Example 5: Finding Empirical Formula

A compound contains 40% carbon, 6.7% hydrogen, and 53.3% oxygen by mass. Find its empirical formula.

Element % ÷ Aᵣ Moles ÷ smallest Ratio
C 40 40÷12 3.33 3.33÷3.33 1
H 6.7 6.7÷1 6.7 6.7÷3.33 2
O 53.3 53.3÷16 3.33 3.33÷3.33 1

Empirical formula: CH₂O

📝 Worked Example 6: Finding Molecular Formula

A compound has empirical formula CH₂O and molecular mass of 180. Find its molecular formula.

Solution:

Empirical formula mass = 12 + (2×1) + 16 = 30

Molecular mass = 180

n = Molecular mass ÷ Empirical formula mass = 180 ÷ 30 = 6

Molecular formula = (CH₂O)₆

Molecular formula: C₆H₁₂O₆

Practice Problems

1. How many moles are in 88 g of carbon dioxide (CO₂)?

M(CO₂) = 12 + (2×16) = 44 g/mol

n = m/M = 88/44 = 2 mol

Answer: 2 moles

2. Calculate the number of molecules in 0.5 moles of water.

Number of molecules = n × L

= 0.5 × 6.02 × 10²³

= 3.01 × 10²³ molecules

3. What mass of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) contains 0.2 moles?

M(CaCO₃) = 40 + 12 + (3×16) = 100 g/mol

m = n × M = 0.2 × 100 = 20 g

Answer: 20 g

4. Iron reacts with chlorine to form iron(III) chloride. Calculate the mass of iron(III) chloride formed when 5.6 g of iron reacts completely with chlorine.

Equation: 2Fe + 3Cl₂ → 2FeCl₃

Mole ratio: 2 mol Fe : 2 mol FeCl₃ (1:1)

n(Fe) = 5.6 ÷ 56 = 0.1 mol

n(FeCl₃) = 0.1 mol

M(FeCl₃) = 56 + (3×35.5) = 162.5 g/mol

m(FeCl₃) = 0.1 × 162.5 = 16.25 g

Answer: 16.25 g of FeCl₃

5. A compound contains 27.3% carbon and 72.7% oxygen by mass. Find its empirical formula.

C: 27.3 ÷ 12 = 2.28 mol

O: 72.7 ÷ 16 = 4.54 mol

Divide by smallest (2.28):

C: 2.28 ÷ 2.28 = 1

O: 4.54 ÷ 2.28 = 2

Empirical formula: CO₂

Real-World Applications

Pharmaceutical Industry

Chemists use stoichiometry to calculate exact amounts of reactants needed to produce medicines, ensuring purity and avoiding waste.

Cooking & Baking

Baking soda (NaHCO₃) reacts with acids in recipes. The mole concept helps understand proper proportions for the right rise!

Fuel Combustion

Engineers use stoichiometry to calculate air-fuel ratios for efficient combustion in engines and power plants.

Environmental Science

Scientists calculate CO₂ emissions from fossil fuels using mole relationships to assess environmental impact.

Key Takeaways for WAEC

  • Memorize Avogadro's number: 6.02 × 10²³
  • Practice the mole triangle: n = m/M
  • Always write balanced equations for stoichiometry
  • Use mole ratios from balanced equations
  • For empirical formula: convert % to moles, then find simplest ratio
  • Molecular formula = (Empirical formula)ₙ where n = Mᵣ ÷ empirical formula mass
