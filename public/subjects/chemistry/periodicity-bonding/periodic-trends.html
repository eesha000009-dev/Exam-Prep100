Introduction to Periodic Trends

The periodic table arranges elements in order of increasing atomic number. This arrangement reveals regular patterns or trends in the properties of elements. These trends exist because elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons, while elements in the same period have the same number of electron shells.

Understanding periodic trends allows us to predict the properties of elements we haven't studied yet!

1. Atomic Radius

The atomic radius is a measure of the size of an atom, typically the distance from the nucleus to the outermost electrons.

Across a Period (→)

Atomic radius DECREASES

  • Nuclear charge increases (more protons)
  • Same number of electron shells
  • Stronger attraction pulls electrons closer

Down a Group (↓)

Atomic radius INCREASES

  • More electron shells are added
  • Outer electrons further from nucleus
  • Inner shells shield outer electrons

2. Ionization Energy

The ionization energy is the minimum energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron from a gaseous atom.

X(g) → X⁺(g) + e⁻

First Ionization Energy

Across a Period (→)

Ionization energy INCREASES

  • Nuclear charge increases
  • Atomic radius decreases
  • Electrons held more tightly
  • Harder to remove electrons

Down a Group (↓)

Ionization energy DECREASES

  • Atomic radius increases
  • Outer electrons further from nucleus
  • Shielding effect increases
  • Easier to remove electrons

Important Exceptions!

There are small drops in ionization energy between:

  • Group 2 → Group 3: Be to B (electron removed from higher energy p-orbital)
  • Group 5 → Group 6: N to O (electron removed from paired electron, more repulsion)

3. Electronegativity

Electronegativity is a measure of the tendency of an atom to attract a bonding pair of electrons. It is measured on the Pauling scale (0 to 4).

Across a Period (→)

Electronegativity INCREASES

  • Nuclear charge increases
  • Atomic radius decreases
  • Stronger attraction for electrons

Down a Group (↓)

Electronegativity DECREASES

  • Atomic radius increases
  • Bonding electrons further from nucleus
  • Increased shielding effect

Key Points

  • Fluorine (F) is the most electronegative element (4.0)
  • Francium (Fr) is the least electronegative (0.7)
  • Metals generally have low electronegativity
  • Non-metals generally have high electronegativity

4. Electron Affinity

Electron affinity is the energy change when an electron is added to a neutral gaseous atom to form a negative ion.

X(g) + e⁻ → X⁻(g)

Energy released = Electron Affinity

Across a Period (→)

Electron affinity becomes more negative (more energy released)

Atoms more eager to gain electrons to achieve stable octet.

Down a Group (↓)

Electron affinity becomes less negative (less energy released)

Larger atomic radius means added electron is further from nucleus.

Summary of Periodic Trends

Property Across Period (→) Down Group (↓) Reason
Atomic Radius Decreases ↓ Increases ↑ Nuclear charge vs. electron shells
Ionization Energy Increases ↑ Decreases ↓ Nuclear attraction vs. distance
Electronegativity Increases ↑ Decreases ↓ Nuclear attraction for bonding electrons
Electron Affinity More negative ↑ Less negative ↓ Energy released on gaining electron

The Shielding Effect

The shielding effect (or screening effect) is the reduction in the effective nuclear charge experienced by outer electrons due to repulsion by inner shell electrons.

Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff)

The effective nuclear charge is the net positive charge experienced by an electron:

Zeff = Z − S

Where Z = atomic number, S = shielding constant

Impact on Trends

  • Shielding increases down a group (more inner shells)
  • Shielding stays relatively constant across a period (same shells)
  • Greater shielding → outer electrons held less tightly
  • This explains why ionization energy decreases down groups

Practice Problems

1. Which element has the largest atomic radius: Na, K, or Li? Explain your answer.

Potassium (K) has the largest atomic radius.

All three are in Group 1. Going down the group, more electron shells are added, making the atom larger. K has more shells than Na, which has more than Li.

2. Explain why fluorine has a higher ionization energy than oxygen.

Fluorine is further right in Period 2, so it has a greater nuclear charge (9 protons vs. 8). The electrons are held more tightly in F, making it harder to remove an electron. Also, F has a smaller atomic radius, meaning electrons are closer to the nucleus.

3. Arrange in order of increasing electronegativity: Cl, Br, I, F

I < Br < Cl < F

These are Group 17 elements (halogens). Electronegativity increases up the group (smaller atomic radius, stronger nuclear attraction for bonding electrons).

Values: I (2.5) < Br (2.8) < Cl (3.0) < F (4.0)

4. Why is the first ionization energy of aluminum lower than that of magnesium?

This is an exception to the general trend. Mg (2,8,2) has its outer electrons in the 3s orbital, while Al (2,8,3) has its outer electron in the 3p orbital.

The 3p electron in Al is:

  • In a higher energy level than 3s
  • Shielded by the 3s² electrons
  • Easier to remove than Mg's 3s electron

5. Predict which element in Period 3 has the highest melting point and explain why.

Silicon (Si) has the highest melting point in Period 3.

Silicon has a giant covalent (macromolecular) structure with strong covalent bonds throughout. This requires a lot of energy to break.

Na, Mg, Al have metallic bonding (weaker than Si's covalent network), while P, S, Cl, Ar have molecular structures with weak intermolecular forces.

Key Takeaways for WAEC

  • Know all four trends: atomic radius, ionization energy, electronegativity, electron affinity
  • Be able to explain WHY each trend occurs
  • Remember the exceptions in ionization energy (Groups 2→3 and 5→6)
  • Fluorine is most electronegative; Francium is least
  • Understand shielding effect and its influence on trends
