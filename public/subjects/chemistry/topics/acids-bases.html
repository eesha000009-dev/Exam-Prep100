1. What are Acids and Bases?

Acids and bases are two important classes of chemical compounds that have significant roles in chemistry, biology, and industry. Understanding their properties and behavior is fundamental to many areas of science.

1.1 Definition of Acids

An acid is a substance that:

  • Tastes sour (but never taste chemicals in the lab!)
  • Turns blue litmus paper red
  • Reacts with metals to produce hydrogen gas
  • Reacts with bases to form salt and water (neutralization)
  • Has a pH less than 7

Examples of acids: Hydrochloric acid (HCl), Sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄), Citric acid (in lemons), Acetic acid (in vinegar)

1.2 Definition of Bases

A base is a substance that:

  • Tastes bitter
  • Feels soapy or slippery
  • Turns red litmus paper blue
  • Reacts with acids to form salt and water
  • Has a pH greater than 7

Examples of bases: Sodium hydroxide (NaOH), Potassium hydroxide (KOH), Ammonia solution (NH₃), Calcium hydroxide (lime water)

2. Theories of Acids and Bases

2.1 Arrhenius Theory (1884)

According to Svante Arrhenius:

  • Acid: A substance that produces H⁺ ions when dissolved in water
  • Base: A substance that produces OH⁻ ions when dissolved in water

Example:

HCl(aq) → H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) (Acid)
NaOH(aq) → Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) (Base)

2.2 Bronsted-Lowry Theory (1923)

This theory provides a broader definition:

  • Acid: A proton (H⁺) donor
  • Base: A proton (H⁺) acceptor

2.3 Lewis Theory (1923)

Gilbert Lewis proposed an even broader definition:

  • Acid: An electron pair acceptor
  • Base: An electron pair donor

3. The pH Scale

The pH scale is a measure of the acidity or alkalinity of a solution. It ranges from 0 to 14:

pH 0-6

Acidic

pH 7

Neutral

pH 8-14

Alkaline

The pH is calculated using: pH = -log[H⁺]

4. Neutralization Reactions

When an acid reacts with a base, they neutralize each other to form salt and water:

Acid + Base → Salt + Water
HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

5. Summary

Litmus test Blue → Red Red → Blue
Taste Sour Bitter
